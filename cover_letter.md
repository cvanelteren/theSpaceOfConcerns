We are pleased to submit our Research Article, “Structuring International Governance through the Space of Concerns,” for consideration at Science.

Consensus-governed institutions present a general measurement problem for computational social science and complex systems research. When decisions are formally unanimous, the official record reveals little about who shaped the agenda, which concerns became linked, or where institutional pressure accumulated — precisely the questions that matter for understanding how cooperation evolves in nested governance settings [2] and how scientific expertise is channelled into policy [3]. We introduce a quantitative framework for recovering this hidden structure directly from an institution’s documentary record. Extending the “product space” framework from economic complexity, which infers latent capabilities from patterns of specialization [1], we reconstruct a relational map of institutional attention from what actors repeatedly prioritize in their submissions, rather than from votes, treaty text, or collaboration networks.

The Antarctic Treaty System provides a stringent test case for this approach. Its consensus rule suppresses the voting and conflict signals on which many existing methods rely, while its six-decade archive is unusually exhaustive and consistently classified. The documentary record is therefore both the principal available trace of institutional positioning and one of the rare cases where such a framework can be evaluated at system scale. We build the map from 6,591 Treaty documents from 1961 to 2025, spanning 66 actors and 45 institution-defined topics, and show that subsequent agenda movement follows its topology.

Four findings emerge. Treaty attention is organized around a dense procedural–environmental core, from which bounded pathways lead toward frontier concerns such as resource extraction, strategic planning, and tourism. Agenda evolution is local and path-dependent: actors move into nearby concerns, and a simple retain-and-adopt process reproduces the system’s structure without broad diffusion. Early entry into emerging concerns is explained less by geopolitical bloc membership than by portfolios that are both broad enough to create routes through the agenda and anchored enough to connect novelty to established procedures. Finally, above-average topic specialization predicts formal policy output: specialist actor–topic cells produce binding Measures at nearly five times the rate of non-specialist cells (OR = 4.96; 95% CI [2.53, 9.73]), while showing no comparable association with procedural Decisions. Together, these results make pre-decisional agenda structure observable in a setting designed to obscure it.

Antarctica is one of the few global commons governed collectively in the name of peace and science, by an assembly of states spanning every continent, making the regime itself of broad and timely interest. But the contribution is more general: we offer a measurement principle for consensus institutions, with the Antarctic Treaty System as proof of concept. The same approach is portable to other document-rich governance arenas, from climate negotiations to the law of the sea and global health, where final agreements understate the structured positioning that produced them, and where the documentary record is often the richest available trace of that process.
