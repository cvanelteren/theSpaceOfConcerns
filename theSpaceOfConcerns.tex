\begin{abstract}
    Consensus institutions can hide strategic differentiation because formal unanimity obscures disagreement in official outcomes. Using six decades of Antarctic Treaty submissions, we infer actor-level activity fingerprints from revealed topic priorities and build a ``space of concerns'' in which institution-defined topics are linked when actors recurrently co-specialize in them. The resulting topology organizes actors into three recurring engagement regimes. Portfolio change is local and path dependent: adoption odds decline with concern-space distance, regime shifts are mostly within or adjacent, and early agenda entry is associated with both broader portfolios and stronger anchoring within one regime rather than with membership in any single regime. A simple split-support mechanism, conditioned on the observed active actors, budgets, and pooled concern space, closely reproduces mean breadth, topic popularity, and local entry patterns. The space of concerns thus provides a behavioral map of how consensus governance organizes and absorbs strategic pressure.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Consensus regimes are often treated as low-conflict settings because formal unanimity masks disagreement in official outcomes. Yet in practice, contestation is displaced into agenda-setting, linkage, and selective engagement \cite{Stone2013,Abbott2015,Hurd2015,Gardiner2025,Drubel2023HiddenContestation}. The core empirical problem is therefore methodological: how do we detect strategic behavior when formal records appear procedural and voting signals are absent?

We address this by shifting attention from collaboration ties to revealed issue activity. Rather than inferring strategy from who collaborates with whom, we extract each actor's activity fingerprint from the \emph{institution’s own topic categories} that they repeatedly prioritize in the forum (as labeled in the ATS document database). We then ask: what behavioral issue topology emerges from co-specialization in these categories, how does it evolve, and how do actors move through it under consensus constraints?

The Antarctic Treaty System (ATS) is an ideal case for this question. Antarctica has no permanent population, but it carries scientific value, strategic significance, and long-standing resource potential. The ATS emerged in 1959 to demilitarize the continent and suspend sovereignty conflict, and it has since broadened into a governance arena for science, environmental protection, logistics, tourism, and resource-related questions \cite{AntarcticTreaty1959,NTI2024,BASTreatyExplained,Joyner1998,Dodds2019,Yao2021}. Yet strategic pressures around militarization and resource access persist and often surface indirectly through science, logistics, and compliance files \cite{DoddsBoulegue2023,Brady2017,Haward2012,Szpak2025}. The archive itself is uneven across both topics and time, with submission volume rising sharply after the 1990s and concentrating in a subset of recurring issue categories (Appendix \cref{fig:topic-submission-overview}). What enters (or stays off) the ATS agenda is therefore a critical site of strategic maneuver with consequences that extend well beyond the continent: what happens in Antarctica does not stay in Antarctica \cite{Guterres2023}.

Our approach adapts economic complexity tools to governance \cite{Hidalgo2007,Orsini2019}. Using revealed policy advantage, we link \emph{institution-defined} topics that members jointly specialize in to build a \emph{space of concerns}, a co-specialization map where distance reflects shared engagement in ATS categories, not linguistic similarity. Because topics are not derived from NLP or topic models, the geometry reflects patterned participation within the institution’s own issue taxonomy. This map makes consensus politics empirically legible in three ways. First, it shows how the ATS tends to channel historically sensitive matters (demilitarization, extraction, tourism access) through a procedural-environmental core rather than direct confrontation. Second, it supports temporal analysis: hazard models reveal that new attention tends to move locally within the space, indicating structured, path-dependent agenda shifts. Third, it enables strategic profiling: regime position and regime anchoring reveal recurring engagement modes and how actors time entry from those positions.

Substantively, we find a dense procedural–environmental hub with distinct arms toward extraction/science, tourism/visitation, and coordination, and we show that participation growth coincides with tighter structural alignment rather than fragmentation. Methodologically, the space of concerns suggests one way to study how consensus regimes filter conflict into structured issue pathways with identifiable boundary zones—useful for monitoring resilience, anticipating pressure points, and motivating comparison across consensus-based institutions beyond Antarctica.

\subsection{The Geometry of Diplomatic Attention}
The space of concerns in \cref{fig:the_space_of_concerns} is a numerically constructed proximity network based on co-specialization: edges link topic pairs for which at least one member specializes in both (RPA $>$ 1 in each), so distance reflects recurrent co-engagement rather than semantic similarity. For exposition, we use qualitative labels (e.g., ``core'', ``corridor'', ``chain'') to describe visually dense or elongated regions of this network; these labels are interpretive and are not themselves model-estimated clusters.

Under this interpretive reading, a dense procedural--environmental zone (opening statements, exchange of information, environmental monitoring/reporting, environmental protection, liability, inspections) sits near the center, with thinner extensions toward resource/science frontiers, tourism/visitation, and operations/planning.

This geometry is consistent with two defining ATS constraints. The 1959 Antarctic Treaty demilitarized the continent, channeling strategic contestation into procedure and science \cite{AntarcticTreaty1959,Joyner1998,Yao2021}, while the 1991 Madrid Protocol prohibited mineral resource activities but allowed tightly regulated scientific drilling \cite{MadridProtocol1991,Koivurova2005}. In the map, this appears as a resource/science corridor linking management and assessment topics to environmental domains analysis, marine acoustics, drilling, and sub-glacial lakes. We interpret this corridor as a dual-use governance zone: capabilities with legitimate scientific value but potential strategic spillovers remain institutionally tethered to core procedural nodes where they can be monitored and negotiated \cite{Sampaio2022,Aus2025,DoddsBoulegue2023,Szpak2025}. Bioprospecting sits closer to core procedural topics than to the corridor's extraction-end topics (especially Drilling and Sub glacial Lakes), which fits this logic.

Bridge topics structure movement between core and periphery. Inspections function as a compliance hinge, while Human Footprint and wilderness values and Marine Protected Areas connect the core to an access-oriented chain (tourism and NGO activities, prevention of marine pollution, site guidelines for visitors, and search and rescue), indicating that access pressures are mediated through conservation and safety obligations rather than overt distributive bargaining \cite{Haward2012,CCAMLRConsensus2022}. Climate change and CEP strategy appear as adjacent connectors rather than detached outliers. In the full concern space, the topology is connected but bounded: only 37 of 1,035 topic pairs have zero proximity (3.57\%), no topic is fully isolated, and the zero-proximity ties are concentrated in a small set of boundary topics, especially Sub glacial Lakes, State of the Antarctic Environment Report SAER, Environmental Domains Analysis, Drilling, Marine Protected Areas, and Prevention of marine pollution (Appendix \cref{fig:heatmap_concerns}). A full ribbon view of all actor-topic supports with RPA $> 1$ across adjacent periods is shown in Appendix \cref{fig:space-ribbon-full}; ribbon width reflects weighted country support rather than static edge magnitude. Simple network summaries point in the same direction: density and giant-component size increase as participation expands, while clustering and betweenness remain low, indicating a broadly connected space rather than tightening local cliques or single-topic brokerage; a greedy member-removal sensitivity check likewise remains modest until late in the deletion sequence, suggesting that the geometry is not driven by any single actor (Appendix \cref{fig:space_network_metrics,fig:sensitivity-analysis}).



\subsection{Modes of Engagement and Actor Strategies}
The Space of Concerns reveals how members allocate attention across issues and how they position themselves in ATS governance. As shown in \cref{fig:portfolio_space_ridgelines}, attention is uneven: countries concentrate in distinct parts of the issue space, and those clusters define three broad engagement regimes. Importantly, the 1D MDS axis is an embedding for visualization rather than a uniform metric ruler: local neighborhood structure is preserved approximately, but dense portions of the network appear compressed while sparse portions are expanded.

Taken together, the regimes form a gradient rather than isolated silos. Regime 1 (institutional coordination and information exchange) emphasizes opening statements, science and education, information exchange, and ATS reporting and operational topics. Regime 2 (compliance and environmental management) centers on environmental protection, EIA, liability, waste and pollution, legal-institutional matters, inspections, and protected-area governance. Regime 3 (frontier impacts, resources, and strategic planning) concentrates on drilling, mineral resources, tourism pressure, SAER/environmental domains, marine acoustics, sub-glacial lakes, CEP strategy, and multiyear planning, where scientific capability, environmental risk, and long-horizon positioning intersect.

These regimes capture dominant orientations, not fixed blocs. Members most strongly aligned with Regime 1 include Switzerland, Malaysia, Estonia, and Bulgaria; with Regime 2, UNEP, COMNAP, Belarus, and IUCN; and with Regime 3, the Russian Federation, the United States, New Zealand, and Australia (Appendix Table~\ref{tab:regime_alignment_full}). Some countries remain tightly concentrated in one region, while others maintain mixed portfolios across neighboring regions, separating structural position from strategic flexibility as agendas shift.

Australia illustrates this distinction: although its portfolio spans all three regions, its center of gravity remains in Regime 3 (share $=0.553$), indicating a diversified but not genuinely between-regime position (Appendix Table~\ref{tab:regime_alignment_full}).

Supplementary co-sponsorship and competition views are consistent with this regime picture. Actor communities concentrate unevenly across the same concern regions, and topic-level concentration remains stable even as participation expands, indicating layered rather than homogenizing growth in agenda engagement (Appendix \cref{fig:actors_on_space,fig:competition-appendix}).

\subsection{Growth of Interest Portfolios is not Random Drift but Local Movement in the Concern Space}
The regime map above is descriptive; the hazard model tests whether movement through this space is structured. We find a strong locality gradient: in the pooled specification, adoption odds fall as concern-space distance increases ($\beta_{\mathrm{distance}}=-0.652$, odds ratio $=0.52$). The empirical adoption curve in \cref{fig:hazard-distance-timeseries} shows the same pattern nonparametrically, with adoption declining from 8.43\% in the nearest distance quartile to 0.79\% in the farthest quartile. The distance effect remains negative under alternative space constructions (instantaneous: $-0.539$; cumulative-lagged: $-0.533$; Appendix \cref{fig:hazard-space-sensitivity}). Consistent with this locality, dominant-regime assignments are highly persistent in rolling 5-year windows: 82.0\% of member transitions remain in the same regime, 99.0\% remain in the same or an adjacent regime, and direct Regime 1$\leftrightarrow$Regime 3 jumps are rare (1.0\%). A direct 1-year versus 5-year comparison (Appendix \cref{fig:regime-window-sensitivity}) shows the expected smoothing: same-regime persistence rises from 58.6\% to 82.0\%, while direct Regime 1$\leftrightarrow$Regime 3 jumps fall from 2.7\% to 1.0\%. Together, these results support interpreting engagement modes as path-dependent orientations rather than static labels, because actors tend to extend portfolios into nearby concerns rather than making long-range jumps.

\subsection{Local Entry and Selective Persistence Recover ATS Portfolio Structure}

The hazard results establish local movement, but they do not show whether that mechanism is strong enough to recover the observed organization of ATS portfolios. We therefore fit a simple split-support model that holds fixed the observed active actor set in each year, the actor-specific budgets they deploy, and a pooled concern-space geometry, then asks whether portfolio dynamics alone can reproduce the observed system. The model updates portfolios in three steps: actors retain part of their prior support, add new topics locally in the concern space, and allocate effort within that selected support. Despite these restrictions, the model reproduces the observed ATS closely: it matches mean actor breadth (observed $3.35$, simulated $3.32$), mean topic popularity (observed $2.45$, simulated $2.45$), annual variation in breadth ($r=0.87$), annual variation in topic popularity ($r=0.98$), and local entry rank in $\phi$-space (observed $0.677$, simulated $0.679$; \cref{fig:split-support-model}). Across 64 repeated simulations with the fitted parameters held fixed, the 5--95\% process band remains centered on the observed series and the split model's local-entry rank stays in a narrow interval around the observed value ($0.662$--$0.688$ versus $0.677$; \cref{fig:split-support-model}). Approximate parameter intervals remain clearly positive for allocation persistence, local fit, entry locality, and retention on prior portfolio weight (Appendix \cref{fig:split-support-params}). Repeated year-balanced thinning of the raw corpus leaves this pattern intact: at 40--80\% retention of submissions, breadth-tracking correlations remain between $0.78$ and $0.86$, and local-entry rank stays within $0.016$ of the observed value (Appendix \cref{fig:split-support-thinning}). This is a mechanism check rather than a full theory of ATS evolution, since participation, budget formation, and possible changes in the concern space are held outside the model. The substantive implication is that much of the observed ATS can be generated by selective persistence, local entry, and constrained allocation, rather than by smooth diffusion across the full issue topology.

\subsubsection{Agenda Pioneering Reflects Breadth and Regime Anchoring}
Early agenda entry is not concentrated in one dominant regime. Actors from all three regimes appear among the pioneers, but earlier movers tend to combine broader portfolios with stronger concentration within one regime rather than evenly mixed portfolios. A simple OLS using the relative pioneer index as the outcome shows that breadth and max regime share are both positive predictors of earlier entry ($\beta_{\mathrm{breadth}}=0.26$, $p=0.001$; $\beta_{\mathrm{anchor}}=17.70$, $p<0.001$), while dominant-regime indicators are not distinguishable from zero. This pattern is stable to alternative cumulative-volume thresholds for defining topic emergence (Appendix \cref{fig:emergence-threshold-sensitivity}). Pioneering therefore appears less as membership in one privileged bloc than as regime-positioned timing from a clear portfolio base.

\section{Discussion}
Consensus regimes are often treated as low-conflict settings, as formal unanimity in official outcomes obscures underlying disagreement. In practice, however, contestation is displaced into agenda-setting, issue linkage, and selective engagement, allowing actors to shape collective trajectories without overtly contesting decisions on record \cite{Stone2013,Hurd2015,Abbott2015,Drubel2023HiddenContestation}. The space of concerns makes that displaced competition observable by tracking revealed activity across topics, rather than relying on formal voting outcomes or collaboration ties alone.

This contribution complements, rather than replaces, existing social-science approaches. Legal-institutional analyses explain mandate design and norm development; diplomatic-history work explains sequencing and practice; co-sponsorship networks explain who aligns with whom \cite{Keohane1984,Dodds2019,Yao2021,Sampaio2022}. Our added value is different: we recover where actors place attention in the issue topology, how that topology is structured, and how actors move through it over time.

Substantively, the ATS appears as a structured concern space organized around a dense procedural--environmental hub, with more specialized but still connected regions around extraction and science, tourism and visitation, and coordination. This topology does not imply a detached extraction arena; rather, it is consistent with an institutional setting in which historically contentious issues, especially resource extraction, are tightly constrained and therefore remain embedded in adjacent procedural, scientific, and environmental pathways. That reading is consistent with current scholarship on dual-use pressures in Antarctica and the persistence of geopolitical tension under a formally cooperative regime \cite{Brady2017,DoddsBoulegue2023,Aus2025,Szpak2025}.

The temporal results strengthen this interpretation. New attention does not diffuse randomly; it tends to move locally in the concern space, and actor trajectories cluster into a small number of recurrent engagement regimes. This indicates structured adaptation rather than drift. In practical terms, change in the ATS is more likely to appear as recombination of adjacent concerns than as abrupt displacement to distant issues. The regime diagnostics do not imply hard blocs, but they do support a stable and policy-interpretable three-regime summary for the present data. The mechanism check reported in the Results is consistent with this reading: ATS portfolios are better described by selective persistence and local expansion within a shared issue arena than by smooth diffusion across the full topology.

These findings also sharpen how consensus stability should be interpreted. Relative institutional stability does not imply absence of strategic pressure; it can reflect effective filtering of disagreement into recurrent issue pathways with stable boundary zones \cite{CCAMLRConsensus2022,Gardiner2025}. Influence in such systems is therefore expressed through portfolio positioning and timing of entry into gateway concerns, not only through activity volume or economic size.

For policy practice, the map can be used as an early-warning layer. As pressures intensify around tourism, climate adaptation, logistics, and infrastructure, likely contention points should emerge first near existing high-salience clusters, especially around inspections, reporting, and management instruments that connect multiple branches of the system \cite{Haward2012,Gardiner2025}. More broadly, this strategy suggests a tractable way to examine other consensus-based institutions where formal agreement conceals strategic differentiation \cite{Vabulas2013,Abbott2015}, though that extension remains to be demonstrated directly.

\section{Methods and Data}
\subsection{Data Description and Processing}
We analyze working papers and information documents submitted to the Antarctic Treaty System (ATS) from 1961 to 2025. The corpus was compiled from the official ATS document archive and enriched with thematic labels, submitting parties, and document types \cite{AntarcticTreatyHandbook,Yaryab2024}. Documents submitted by the ATS Secretariat were retained for completeness.

Data collection was performed on 6 February 2026 from the ATS website (\url{https://www.ats.aq/devAS/Meetings/DocDatabase?lang=e}) using the antarctic-database-go collector by Carlo Hamalainen (\url{https://github.com/carlohamalainen/antarctic-database-go}). A key property of this framework is reproducibility: it stores cached HTTP responses and local copies of retrieved source documents, records processing status in SQLite, and supports deterministic re-runs and auditability of the extraction pipeline. That means that if the website changes or documents are removed, the collected dataset can be reconstructed from the cached responses, ensuring that the analysis can be replicated even if the source data evolves.

The category labels are Secretariat-assigned (not NLP-derived) and thus the space reflects co-participation within the ATS’s own issue taxonomy rather than semantic similarity. Because agenda items vary across meetings over time, the Secretariat created cross-temporal categories to provide a harmonized, consistent classification of documents across the archive (ATS correspondence). For preprocessing, we expanded multi-party entries into one row per submitting actor and deduplicated attachment-linked records using unique \texttt{paper\_id}, yielding 6,591 unique papers in the 1961--2025 analysis panel. We also excluded records labeled \texttt{ALL}, which were largely uncategorized and introduced noise into topic assignment (especially in recent years). Our unit of analysis is the \emph{submitting actor}, not legal status within the Treaty. We therefore retain consultative parties, non-consultative parties, observers, expert bodies, and Secretariat submissions whenever they appear in the document archive. The reason is substantive: this paper studies issue positioning and agenda formation in the ATS document system, not formal voting power at the final consensus stage. These actor types are not institutionally equivalent, but they all contribute to what enters the agenda, how issues are framed, and which concerns are repeatedly linked in practice. Mixing them would be inappropriate for a model of formal decision authority; it is appropriate for a model of revealed issue activity in the broader ATS arena \cite{Sampaio2022,AntarcticTreatyHandbook}.


\subsection{Constructing the Space of Concerns}
Let $x(a,i)$ denote the number of submissions by actor $a$ on topic $i$, where actors include consultative parties, non-consultative parties, observers, relevant organizations, and the ATS Secretariat whenever they appear as submitters in the archive. We define the normalized activity share
\begin{equation}\label{eq:activity_share}
\pi(a,i) = \frac{x(a,i)}{\sum_j x(a,j)},
\end{equation}

and the system-wide importance of topic $i$ as

\begin{equation}\label{eq:topic_importance}
Z_i = \frac{\sum_c x(c,i)}{\sum_{c,j} x(c,j)}.
\end{equation}

The Revealed Policy Advantage (RPA; an RCA-style specialization index) is then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rpa}
    \rho(a,i) = \frac{\pi(a,i)}{Z_i}.
\end{equation}

Topics are linked by proximity
\begin{equation}\label{eq:proximity}
\phi(i,j)
=
\min\!\left\{
\begin{aligned}
&\Pr\!\bigl(\rho(a,i) > 1 \mid \rho(a,j) > 1\bigr),\\
&\Pr\!\bigl(\rho(a,j) > 1 \mid \rho(a,i) > 1\bigr)
\end{aligned}
\right\}.
\end{equation}
yielding a weighted topic network. Taking the minimum of the two conditional probabilities serves three purposes. First, using conditional rather than joint probability controls for differences in topic prevalence: two relatively rare topics can still register as close if specialization in one reliably coincides with specialization in the other, whereas a joint probability would mechanically shrink with rarity. Second, taking the minimum prevents one-way dependence from overstating proximity. If a niche topic almost always appears alongside a broad topic, ($P(\text{broad}\mid \text{niche})$) may be high even when the reverse is low; the minimum forces the proximity to be limited by that weaker direction. Third, this yields a symmetric measure of relatedness, so the link between topics reflects mutual co-specialization rather than directional inclusion. Probabilities are estimated empirically across members within the relevant analysis window.
We construct both an aggregate space (for the regime-wide map) and time-resolved spaces by rebuilding proximities within each time window, allowing the topology itself to evolve rather than assuming a fixed structure over time.
This adapts the product-space proximity logic from economic complexity to governance concerns \cite{Hidalgo2007}.

\subsection{Hazard Model for Local Topic Adoption}
To test whether agenda expansion is local in the concern space, we estimate a rolling member-level hazard model on adjacent annual windows. For each member $a$, topic $i$, and transition $t-1 \rightarrow t$, an observation is included only if the topic is at risk:
\[
\text{at-risk}_{a i t}=1 \iff \rho_{a i, t-1} < 1.
\]
The binary outcome is then
\[
y_{a i t}=1 \iff \rho_{a i, t} \ge 1 \ \text{and}\ \rho_{a i, t-1} < 1.
\]
Within each member-window panel we fit a logistic model with standardized covariates
\begin{dmath}
\Pr(y_{a i t}=1)=\mathrm{logit}^{-1}\!\left(
\beta_0
\!+\!\beta_d\,\tilde d_{a i, t-1}
\!+\!\beta_b\,\widetilde{\mathrm{breadth}}_{a,t-1}
\!+\!\beta_p\,\widetilde{\mathrm{pop}}_{i,t-1}
\right),
\end{dmath}
where $d_{a i, t-1}$ is the minimum concern-space distance from topic $i$ to the member's previously active topics, $\mathrm{breadth}_{a,t-1}$ is the number of previously active topics, and $\mathrm{pop}_{i,t-1}$ is prior topic popularity (share of members with $\rho \ge 1$). The plotted heatmap reports $\beta_d$ by member and window; negative values indicate preferential expansion into nearby topics. We summarize each window with the median and interquartile range across members.
In the main specification, distances are computed from the aggregate concern space; Appendix \cref{fig:hazard-space-sensitivity} reports sensitivity to instantaneous and cumulative-lagged spaces.
For regime-persistence diagnostics, we assign each member-window a dominant regime from region shares computed on $\max(\rho-1,0)$ over the fixed three-region topic partition used in \cref{fig:portfolio_space_ridgelines}, then tabulate member transitions across adjacent windows. We report same-regime, adjacent-regime, and far-jump ($1\leftrightarrow3$) transition rates.

\subsection{Sequential Split-Support Model of Portfolio Dynamics}
To test whether the ATS can be approximated by a simple generative mechanism, we fit a reduced-form model conditioned on three observed objects: (i) the active actor set in each year, (ii) actor-specific budgets $K_{at}$, and (iii) a fixed pooled concern-space matrix $\phi$ from \cref{eq:proximity}. The goal is not to explain who becomes active or how budgets arise, but whether the observed organization of interests can be reproduced once those constraints are given.

A single support equation proved too blunt because it conflated topic retention and topic entry. We therefore model portfolio change sequentially. Let $x_{ait}$ denote actor $a$'s submission mass on topic $i$ at time $t$, let $x_{at}$ denote the corresponding topic-allocation vector, and let $K_{at}=\sum_i x_{ait}$ be the observed total budget in that year. Define the support indicator $z_{ait}=1[x_{ait}>0]$ and the support set $S_{at}=\{i:z_{ait}=1\}$. We write $s_{ai,t-1}=x_{ai,t-1}/K_{a,t-1}$ for the prior topic share and $p_{i,t-1}$ for prior topic popularity. The latent Bernoulli draws $z^{\mathrm{ret}}_{ait}$ and $z^{\mathrm{ent}}_{ait}$ indicate whether a previously held topic is retained and whether a previously absent topic is entered, respectively; $\alpha_i^{\mathrm{ret}}$, $\alpha_i^{\mathrm{ent}}$, and $\alpha_i$ are topic-specific intercepts, while $\delta_{\mathrm{ret}}$ and $\delta_{\mathrm{ent}}$ are stage-specific intercept shifts. For topics already in the portfolio, retention is
\begin{dmath}
\Pr\!\left(z^{\mathrm{ret}}_{ait}=1 \mid z_{ai,t-1}=1\right)
=
\mathrm{logit}^{-1}\!\Bigl(
\alpha_i^{\mathrm{ret}}
\!+\!
\delta_{\mathrm{ret}}
\!+\!
\lambda_{\mathrm{ret}} s_{ai,t-1}
\!+\!
\gamma_{\mathrm{ret}} p_{i,t-1}
\Bigr).
\end{dmath}
For topics not yet held, entry is
\begin{dmath}
\Pr\!\left(z^{\mathrm{ent}}_{ait}=1 \mid z_{ai,t-1}=0\right)
=
\mathrm{logit}^{-1}\!\Bigl(
\alpha_i^{\mathrm{ent}}
\!+\!
\delta_{\mathrm{ent}}
\!+\!
\beta_{\mathrm{ent}}\,\mathrm{Fit}_{ait}
\!+\!
\gamma_{\mathrm{ent}} p_{i,t-1}
\Bigr),
\end{dmath}
with
\begin{dmath}
\mathrm{Fit}_{ait}
=
\frac{1}{|S_{a,t-1}|}
\sum_{j\in S_{a,t-1}}
\phi(i,j),
\end{dmath}
so new topics are favored when they are close to the actor's prior support in the native concern space. The same $\mathrm{Fit}_{ait}$ term enters the allocation stage below as the average $\phi$-proximity of candidate topic $i$ to the actor's previous support.

After combining retained and entered topics into the support set $S_{at}$, the observed budget $K_{at}$ is allocated across the selected support by
\begin{dmath}
x_{at} \sim \mathrm{Multinomial}\!\Bigl(K_{at}, \mathrm{softmax}\!\Bigl( \alpha_i \!+\! \rho s_{ai,t-1} \!+\! \beta\,\mathrm{Fit}_{ait} \!+\! \gamma p_{i,t-1}\Bigr)_{i\in S_{at}} \Bigr).
\end{dmath}
Topic intercepts $\alpha_i$, $\alpha_i^{\mathrm{ent}}$, and $\alpha_i^{\mathrm{ret}}$ are empirical baselines estimated from pooled topic mass, pooled entry frequency, and pooled retention frequency. The free coefficients $(\rho,\beta,\gamma)$, $(\delta_{\mathrm{ent}},\beta_{\mathrm{ent}},\gamma_{\mathrm{ent}})$, and $(\delta_{\mathrm{ret}},\lambda_{\mathrm{ret}},\gamma_{\mathrm{ret}})$ are estimated by maximum likelihood. A final scalar intercept calibration is then applied to the entry and retention logits during simulation to align support levels while keeping the fitted slopes fixed.

This sequential split-support model performs well despite its simplicity. The fitted allocation stage remains strongly persistent and local ($\rho=4.29$, $\beta=3.97$), the entry stage is also local ($\beta_{\mathrm{ent}}=0.58$), and retention favors previously important topics ($\lambda_{\mathrm{ret}}=0.42$). After the intercept calibration, the model reproduces mean actor breadth (observed $3.35$, simulated $3.32$), mean topic popularity (observed $2.45$, simulated $2.45$), annual variation in breadth ($r=0.87$), annual variation in topic popularity ($r=0.98$), and local entry rank in $\phi$-space (observed $0.677$, simulated $0.679$). The main implication is that a large share of ATS portfolio structure can be generated by selective persistence, local entry, and constrained allocation, without requiring actors to diffuse uniformly across the issue space.

Approximate parameter intervals tell the same story. In the split model, allocation persistence and local fit remain clearly positive ($\rho=4.29$, 95\% Wald interval $[3.87,4.71]$; $\beta=3.97$, $[2.92,5.02]$), the entry stage remains local ($\beta_{\mathrm{ent}}=0.58$, $[0.19,0.97]$), and retention continues to favor previously important topics ($\lambda_{\mathrm{ret}}=0.42$, $[0.11,0.74]$; \cref{fig:split-support-params}). By contrast, the retention-stage popularity term is imprecise and overlaps zero, suggesting that the robust part of the mechanism lies in persistence, local entry, and prior portfolio weight rather than in a strong retention response to topic-wide popularity.

The model's strong fit could in principle be an artifact of later, denser ATS years. To probe that possibility, we repeated the full fitting and simulation exercise after randomly thinning the raw submissions within each year, rebuilding the actor-topic panels each time while holding the pooled concern space fixed. The split-support model remains the best simple specification across these thinned corpora: at 40--80\% retention of the raw submissions, mean breadth-tracking correlations remain between $0.78$ and $0.86$, above the pooled-support alternative ($0.69$--$0.75$), while local-entry rank stays close to the observed value (difference at most $0.016$; \cref{fig:split-support-thinning}). This suggests that the split result is not simply an artifact of dense late-period data.

\subsection{Pioneering Timing and Regime Position}
To characterize how early agenda entry relates to the regime structure, we define a member-level pioneer index and then relate it to breadth and regime position.

First, we establish a topic emergence year for each policy topic. This is defined as the first year in which the cumulative number of submissions on that topic reaches 15\% of its total historical volume. This metric identifies when a topic transitions from a niche issue to part of the mainstream agenda.

Second, we determine a member entry year for each member-topic pair. Using a 5-year rolling window to smooth out short-term fluctuations, we calculate RPA for every member in every topic for each window. A member is considered to have ``entered'' a topic in the first year its RPA value is greater than or equal to 1.0, signifying specialization.

Third, we calculate a relative pioneer index. For each member-topic specialization, we compute a ``relative lag'': the difference between the member's entry year and the topic's emergence year (or the member's first year of activity, whichever is later). A negative lag indicates pioneering behavior, i.e. adopting a topic before it becomes mainstream, while a positive lag indicates a follower. The pioneer index for a given member is the negative of its average relative lag across all topics it specializes in, such that a higher, positive score corresponds to earlier adoption.

Finally, we summarize each member's regime position using the same three-region partition employed in \cref{fig:portfolio_space_ridgelines}. Let $\omega_{ar}$ denote member $a$'s share of excess specialization signal in regime $r$, and let $m_a=\max_r \omega_{ar}$ denote the member's regime anchoring. We then estimate a simple cross-sectional model
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{PioneerIndex}_a = \alpha + \beta\,\mathrm{Breadth}_a + \lambda\,m_a + \delta_{R(a)} + \varepsilon_a,
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{Breadth}_a$ is the number of specialized topics and $R(a)$ is the member's dominant regime. In the fitted data, both breadth ($\beta=0.26$, $p=0.001$) and regime anchoring ($\lambda=17.70$, $p<0.001$) are positively associated with earlier entry, while dominant-regime indicators are not distinguishable from zero. This is why the main-text pioneer result is framed in terms of regime position and anchoring rather than a distinct ``pioneer type'' attached to one regime alone.

\subsection{Sensitivity Analysis}
To evaluate the stability of the observed structure, we performed a sensitivity analysis by removing each member from the space of concerns and computing the root mean squared error between the proximity matrix of the full space and the space without that member. The process is repeated by iteratively removing the member that produces the maximal difference between the current set of members and the set of members without that member. This greedy approach helps us understand what each member contributes to the construction of the space of concerns in Antarctica.

In \cref{fig:sensitivity-analysis}, we observe that the space of concerns is robust against the removal of a large fraction of members. This is evidenced by the relatively gradual increase in spectral distance observed through the first 60\% of removals, suggesting that the global structure of the space is maintained even when many individual contributors are excluded. This robustness implies a degree of redundancy or modularity in how members co-structure the space, where no single actor—apart from a few key exceptions—critically determines the overall topology.

Because early-adoption metrics depend on topic-emergence timing, we also recomputed topic emergence years over cumulative-volume thresholds from 5\% to 30\% (\cref{fig:emergence-threshold-sensitivity}). Median emergence timing remains stable, and shifts relative to the 15\% baseline are generally small across topics, indicating that the pioneer timing results are not driven by a single percentile choice.

However, we also observe distinct inflection points in the curve, where the removal of specific members results in sharp increases in spectral distance. Notably, members such as China, Germany, France, and the United Kingdom contribute disproportionately to the coherence of the product space. Their removal induces substantial distortions in the spectral representation, indicating their role as structural anchors or bridges across different regions of the space. In contrast, even members with large economic output, such as the United States and India, show a more subdued effect on the global geometry, underscoring that structural importance is not merely a function of scale but also of positionality within the network.

Toward the final stages of the removal sequence, the spectral distance rapidly approaches its maximum, signifying a collapse of the original structure. Interestingly, some of the members responsible for this final increase—such as Sweden, Venezuela, and Romania—are not necessarily central by economic metrics but appear to occupy topologically unique or peripheral positions that preserve fragile clusters or minor components.

Overall, the analysis highlights the asymmetric role of members in shaping the global structure of the space of concerns. While many actors contribute incrementally, a small subset exerts an outsized influence on the cohesion and organization of the space. This spectral sensitivity analysis thus offers a principled approach for identifying structurally pivotal actors beyond conventional economic indicators.
