\begin{abstract}
Governance systems operate through the interplay of formal rules and informal influence, yet this relationship is difficult to quantify. We apply economic-complexity tools to the Antarctic Treaty System (ATS) to map what the regime actually does and how actors navigate it. Adapting Revealed Comparative Advantage to working and information papers, we construct a Revealed Policy Advantage measure that normalizes for overall activity and identifies where, rather than how much, states concentrate attention. From these patterns we derive a space of concerns: a product-space-style network in which policy topics are linked by co-specialization. We then use a proximity-weighted diffusion process on this space to infer latent interest profiles and compare them to revealed ones. The resulting excess KL divergence captures how far an actor’s observable activity deviates from the structurally implied profile. Combining this measure with the breadth of each actor’s revealed portfolio, we identify four roles—strategic specialists, aligned specialists, structural generalists, and latent generalists—that describe how states use or underuse their structural position in the issue space. The analysis reveals a structured landscape of concerns in which a small set of topics anchor the regime and some actors occupy narrowly visible yet structurally expansive niches, challenging the view of the ATS as a purely scientific collaboration and demonstrating how economic-complexity methods can uncover hidden patterns of strategic engagement in international governance.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The Antarctic Treaty System stands as one of the most successful examples of international cooperation in modern history, often hailed as the gold standard for global governance and the peaceful resolution of territorial disputes. Established during the Cold War in 1959 by twelve nations and now expanded to 54 parties, it has maintained Antarctica as a zone dedicated to peaceful scientific research for over six decades.

The system's reputation rests on several remarkable accomplishments: it suspended territorial claims without resolving them, established a nuclear-free zone during the nuclear arms race, and created mechanisms for peaceful dispute resolution that have prevented militarization for over half a century \cite{Haward2012, Koivurova2005, Brady2011}. Covering an area roughly the size of Europe, Antarctica represents one of the most challenging governance environments on Earth—a vast, remote continent rich in strategic interests and untapped resources.

Recent developments, however, reveal the system's complexity. In 2019, while scientists from China, the United States, Russia, and European countries collaborated on climate research at Zhongshan Station, the same year saw rising concerns over China's expanding Antarctic presence \cite{Brady2017, Hemmings2017}. This duality of visible cooperation and strategic unease is consistent with broader evidence that international institutions often operate through a mix of formal rules, informal practices, and strategic behaviour \cite{Roger2022, Biersteker2024, Vabulas2013, Barnett2021, Stone2013, Slaughter2004}. Rather than treating the Antarctic Treaty System as an exception to these patterns, we take this evidence as a starting point to ask how strategic calculations manifest within a regime that is widely viewed as a diplomatic success.

The formal record of the Antarctic Treaty System—treaties, recommendations, and meeting reports—captures negotiated outcomes but often not the full process by which they emerge. Informal corridor conversations, long-standing personal ties, and coordination within and across delegations influence which issues reach the agenda, how they are framed, and which proposals gain momentum. Because decision-making is formally unanimous, attribution of objections or blocked consensus to particular states is uneven across meeting reports, so important aspects of contestation and veto power are only partially visible in the record. At the same time, the record captures both positive acts—such as the submission, co-sponsorship, and explicit support of working papers and information documents—and indirect acts, such as the absence of support for a proposal or indirect support through measures like (the threat of) withdrawing funding. These informal dynamics nonetheless leave traces in formal acts, especially in patterns of sponsorship, support, and non-support. Existing work on international organizations underscores the importance of such informal practices and hidden influence, but offers limited tools for systematically relating them to observable patterns of institutional activity \cite{Roger2022, Biersteker2024, Vabulas2013, Barnett2021, Stone2013, Slaughter2004}.

We build on these traces to recover a more fine-grained picture of what the Antarctic Treaty System actually does. We propose to use these formal submissions as observable fingerprints of underlying decision processes. States reveal their interests not only through the topics on which they submit or co-sponsor papers, but also through the topics they systematically ignore. An entity may downplay a politically sensitive concern by concentrating visible activity in neighbouring topics that are closely connected in the space—for example, by focusing on scientific or environmental dimensions that sit adjacent to more contentious issues of resource access or jurisdiction. In such a representation, the proximity of topics to one another matters as much as the clustering of states: topics that lie close together are those that tend to be pursued as a bundle of concerns, while distant topics mark sharper trade-offs.

From these joint patterns of engagement, we construct a \textit{space of concerns}: a low-dimensional representation in which states and topics are positioned according to how they co-occur in treaty practice. In this space, positions and distances summarize revealed priorities and their relationships. Conceptually, this construction is a direct application of Hidalgo et al.'s \textit{product space} framework \cite{Hidalgo2007} to international governance: instead of countries and products linked by revealed comparative advantage, we map states and policy domains linked by revealed policy advantage in Antarctic Treaty practice.

The space of concerns serves three purposes in our analysis. First, it granularizes the Treaty’s broad constitutional narrative into an empirically grounded map of what the regime actually does, topic by topic, translating high-level principles of science, peace, and environmental protection into a concrete landscape of policy work.

Second, it provides a structural context for assessing how specialized or diversified each actor’s engagement is, revealing whether countries occupy narrow niches, central hubs, or peripheral cul-de-sacs in the issue space, and whether they systematically neglect structurally adjacent concerns.

Third, it underpins our classification of actor roles—such as strategic specialists, aligned specialists, structural generalists, and latent generalists—by linking revealed policy advantages to the latent opportunities and constraints implied by the topology of the concern network.

If the Antarctic Treaty System—widely considered the pinnacle of successful international cooperation—exhibits systematic patterns consistent with strategic calculations rather than purely scientific motivations, then this should be visible in how states are located relative to different bundles of topics, in how specialized or generalist their revealed profiles appear in this space, and in how their positions evolve over time.

Our analysis proceeds in three steps. First, we use revealed policy advantages to map where actors concentrate their submissions and how topics are proximally related in the concern space. Second, we examine the resulting topology to identify clusters of issues and structural roles within the network, showing how some concerns anchor the regime while others remain peripheral. Third, we exploit the geometry of this space to infer latent interests via a diffusion process, comparing actors’ revealed profiles to the structurally implied ones and classifying them into strategic specialists, aligned specialists, structural generalists, and latent generalists. If the Antarctic Treaty System—widely considered the pinnacle of successful international cooperation—exhibits systematic patterns consistent with strategic calculations rather than purely scientific motivations, this should be visible in the structure of the concern space, in the distribution of actor roles, and in how these patterns evolve over time.

By constructing and analysing this space of concerns, we show which actors cluster around similar bundles of issues, how they move through neighbouring topics, which dimensions of policy trade-offs dominate their behaviour, and where latent tensions or potential coalitions are likely to emerge. This representation can be used to anticipate where proposals will encounter resistance or support, to identify states whose stated commitments diverge from their revealed priorities, and to detect cases where actors appear to be preparing the ground for future engagement on more contentious topics by first building activity in adjacent areas. More broadly, the approach offers a generalizable tool for diagnosing the alignment between formal commitments and actual practice in complex governance systems, and for assessing how institutional design and geopolitical change reshape the underlying landscape of concerns.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Revealed Policy Advantage and actor specialization}
We treat the Antarctic Treaty System as a set of agents whose interactions are structured by formal rules and informal norms. The observable actions of these agents—above all, their submissions of working papers and information documents—provide a record of how they allocate attention across policy domains. These actions reveal underlying priorities and strategic calculations, which may not always align with their official commitments to scientific cooperation and environmental protection.

These submission patterns provide a rich source of data on how countries prioritize different policy domains. If an agent proposes a working paper on a specific topic, it indicates that the agent has a vested interest in that area (see Figure \ref{fig:co-submission}), whether it be resource management, territorial claims, or scientific research. The question is \textit{how much} interest the agent has in that area relative to other agents and particularly in relation to the overall strategic objectives within the Antarctic Treaty System.

We quantify these revealed interests using the submission patterns of working papers and information documents. Let $x(a, i)$ denote the number of working papers submitted by an agent $a$ on topic $i \in T$. We define the \textit{Revealed Policy Advantage} ($\rho$) for each country and topic as follows:

\begin{dmath} \label{eq:rpa}
  \rho(a,i) = \frac{\pi(a,i)}{Z_i}
\end{dmath}

with $\pi(a,i) = x(a,i) / (\sum_j x(a,j))$ indicating the policy focus of agent $a$ on topic $i$ and $Z_i = \frac{\sum_c x(c,i)}{\sum_{c,j} x(c,j)}$ representing the general interest in topic $i$ across all agents.

For values $\rho(a, i) > 1$ an agent has more vested interest in topic $i$ than the other agents in the system. The revealed policy advantage captures the relative specialization of each agent in different policy domains. This concept is adapted from Revealed Comparative Advantage in economic complexity theory, where countries are evaluated based on their export patterns relative to global trade dynamics \cite{Hidalgo2007}.

Using Equation \ref{eq:rpa}, we construct the relative proximity between topics $i$ and $j$ by considering how likely it is for an agent to be interested in both topics. If an agent submits in topic $i$, it is more likely that they also submit in another topic $j$. We define the proximity between topics $i$ and $j$ as the minimum conditional probability that an agent specializing in one topic also specializes in the other:
\begin{dmath} \label{eq:proximity}
\begin{aligned}
\phi(i,j) = \min_c \{&\\
    & P(\rho(c,i) \ge 1 \mid \rho(c,j) \ge 1),\\
    & P(\rho(c,j) \ge 1 \mid \rho(c,i) \ge 1)\\\}
\end{aligned}
\end{dmath}

This proximity measure captures how likely specialization in one topic is accompanied by specialization in another, revealing the structure of the space of concerns. Taking the minimum of the two conditional probabilities yields a conservative estimate of the relationship and prevents spurious high proximities driven by a single highly specialized agent.

Figure \ref{fig:rca_heatmap} visualizes the revealed policy advantage ($\log \rho(i,c)$) for each agent across different topics. The heatmap illustrates a clear distinction between specialized and diversified actors within the Antarctic Treaty System.

At the top of the heatmap, we observe highly specialized actors with narrow policy focus. These include single-issue members such as IPY-IPO and WTO, as well as countries like Greece, Denmark, and Iceland. Their limited activity concentrates on specific topics, resulting in high revealed policy advantages within those areas. In contrast, countries such as the United States, Australia, and the United Kingdom demonstrate broad engagement across multiple topics, reflecting more diverse policy interests and comprehensive involvement in the treaty system.

The heatmap further reveals distinct clusters of countries with aligned policy interests, notably among European Union members and South American countries, suggesting regional coordination patterns within the broader Antarctic governance framework. The marginal panel plots display average activity levels across agents (right panel) and topics (top panel). These distributions highlight that collaboration sits at the heart of the Antarctic Treaty System, with the majority of topics focusing on long-term planning, environmental domain analysis, marine acoustics, and environmental monitoring.

The panels also reveal that information and working papers show less engagement with direct scientific collaboration, suggesting that institutional practice often centres on governance and strategic concerns even when scientific cooperation is the formal justification.

Climate change emerges as a pivotal issue, creating an even distribution across the topic spectrum and serving as a fulcrum that balances topics above and below the mean activity level. A notable pattern emerges when examining agent activity: countries with generally high activity levels tend to share similar interest profiles. The topic spectrum divides similarly for countries in the lower half of the activity distribution compared to those at the top. This suggests that specialized, single-party issues tend to cover topics that the more active core participants address less frequently, creating a complementary structure within the treaty system.

\subsection{The space of concerns: topic network and structural roles}
From the topic–topic proximities in Equation \ref{eq:proximity}, we construct a network in which nodes are policy domains and weighted edges represent the strength of their association in treaty practice. This network is the operational form of the space of concerns introduced in the Introduction and corresponds to the product-space-style topology induced by revealed policy advantages.

The space of concerns can be used as a map where traversing from one topic to another represents the shortest path between two policy domains implied by the submission patterns of the agents that constitute the Antarctic Treaty System. In Figure \ref{fig:space_of_concerns}, we depict the maximum spanning tree of the space of concerns, which highlights the strongest connections between topics. The maximum spanning tree is constructed by selecting the edges that connect the nodes in such a way that the total weight of the edges is maximized, where the weight of each edge is defined by the proximity between topics.

The maximum spanning tree is not the complete picture. We computed network centrality measures to characterise the structure of the concern network. The proximities were converted to distances, $d(i, j) = \frac{1}{\phi(i,j)}$ with $i, j \in T$, before computing the centrality metrics.

The space of concerns exhibits a mixed structural pattern with both hierarchical and clustered characteristics. Power centrality shows a roughly normal distribution centered around moderate values, indicating relatively balanced connectivity across the network rather than extreme core-periphery polarization (Figure \ref{fig:centrality-ats}). Eigenvector centrality reveals a distinct pattern with most nodes clustered around moderate values (~0.2) and a small number of highly influential outliers near maximum centrality (1.0), suggesting a few dominant concerns that anchor the network's influence structure. Betweenness centrality displays the expected right-skewed distribution with most nodes having very low betweenness values, confirming that only a small subset of concerns serve as critical bridges connecting different parts of the network. Closeness centrality is concentrated in a relatively narrow range (0.5-0.7), indicating that most concerns maintain similar levels of accessibility within the overall network structure, rather than being evenly distributed across all possible values.

\subsection{Inferring hidden interests}
We assume that each agent’s revealed activity $\rho(a, t)$ is a sample from a latent interest distribution $\theta_a$. This distribution reflects both the agent’s intrinsic priorities and the structural proximity between topics. The \protect\textit{space of concerns} captures this structure: it encodes how topics are related based on patterns of co-submission, and defines how influence or relevance might diffuse between them. That is, we assume that if two topics are closely connected in this space, then an agent active in one is more likely to have latent interest in the other. The revealed policy advantage $\rho(a, .)$ serves as an initial estimate of the agent’s latent interest distribution, such that $\hat{\theta}_a = \rho(a, .)$. Combined with the space of concerns, this forms a prior belief from which we infer a more complete profile of the agent’s interests.

Our goal is to infer $\theta_a$ given $\rho(a, .)$, using the structure of the space of concerns to guide the inference. We begin with an initial estimate

\[
  \hat{\theta}_a = \rho(a, .)
\]

and iteratively update this estimate using a diffusion process defined by

\begin{equation}
  f^{(t+1)}_a = \alpha \hat{\theta}_a + (1 - \alpha) D^{-1} W f^{(t)}_a,
  \label{eq:diffusion}
\end{equation}

until convergence, i.e. when $|f^{(t + 1)}_a - f^{(t)}_a| < \epsilon$, with $\epsilon = 1 \times 10^{-6}$ and $f^{(0)} = \hat{\theta}$. Here, $W$ is the weighted adjacency matrix of the topic proximity network, and $D$ is the diagonal degree matrix. The parameter $\alpha$ controls the balance between retention of the revealed signal and structural diffusion: for $\alpha = 1$, no diffusion occurs and the latent profile is equal to the revealed interest, $f_a = \hat{\theta}_a$. In contrast, for $\alpha = 0$, the resulting distribution $f_a$ reflects only the global structure of the topic space and becomes identical for all agents.

This formulation generalizes the PageRank algorithm, which typically sets $\alpha = 0.85$, and allows us to examine how interest distributions evolve as a function of structural proximity. As $\alpha$ decreases, diffusion progresses further over the structure, smoothing and redistributing the agent's revealed focus into adjacent topics. Agents whose revealed interests are structurally aligned will converge quickly toward the structural equilibrium. In contrast, agents whose interests are concentrated in structurally isolated regions will show slower convergence. This divergence reveals latent affinity to nearby concerns that were not explicitly revealed in their original activity.

To control for singular parties having high activity, we use a one-hot encoder to compute a baseline diffusion profile, $f^0_a$, which is the diffusion of a one-hot vector representing the agent's revealed interest. This baseline serves as a reference point for comparing the agent's latent profile against a focused submission. The excess KL divergence between the latent profile and the RCA, normalized against the baseline diffusion, quantifies how much more the agent's RCA diverges from the structure-induced latent profile than would be expected from a focused submission alone.

Figure \ref{fig:hidden_interests} illustrates the hidden interests — operationalized as the KL-divergence between the diffused latent profile and the revealed comparative advantage (RCA) — of each agent in the Antarctic Treaty System. The excess KL divergence is computed as follows: for each agent, we simulate a one-hot RCA vector, representing the most concentrated possible revealed interest. This synthetic baseline is diffused across the space of concerns in the same manner as the true RCA vector. The KL divergence between the diffused one-hot vector and the structure itself serves as a lower-bound reference. The \textit{excess} KL for an agent is then the difference between the KL divergence of their actual profile and that of the one-hot reference. This quantifies how much more an agent’s revealed interests diverge from the structural equilibrium than would be expected from pure specialization alone. High excess KL values thus suggest structurally misaligned or strategically narrow interest profiles, while lower values indicate interests that are already well-aligned with the topology of concerns.

From these profiles, we can coarsely delineated four stylized classes. Agents with a narrow interest range (low RCA entropy) and high excess KL divergence are classified as \textit{strategic specialists}. Their revealed interests are focused, but diffusion reveals that they have latent interests in nearby topics that are not reflected in their submissions. \textit{Aligned specialists} also have narrow interests, but their revealed profile aligns closely with the structural equilibrium, showing little divergence after diffusion. \textit{Structural generalists} exhibit broad, well-aligned interests with minimal divergence, while \textit{latent generalists} appear broad in their RCA, yet diffusion reveals even greater latent reach—suggesting overlooked or unrealized structural potential across the issue space.

\section{The Antarctic Treaty System as a Space of Concerns}
The Antarctic Treaty System (ATS), born out of Cold War, represents a unique governance framework where science and innovation take precedence over strategic or resource-driven interests. Since its inception in the 1950s, the ATS has expanded significantly, with an increasing number of countries joining its ranks. This study introduces the concept of the "space of concerns" to analyze the topics prioritized within the system and their interrelations. By mapping these interrelations, we uncover the structural dynamics of the ATS, revealing a core-periphery structure where a few central topics are highly interconnected with numerous peripheral ones.

The space of concerns serves as a lens to explore how countries navigate the governance landscape of Antarctica. By quantifying the overt interests of countries through their submissions of information and working papers, we can trace how interest in one policy domain flows to related domains. This framework not only highlights the uneven distribution of interests—where some countries and topics dominate—but also provides insights into the systemic interplay of priorities within the ATS.

We characterized potential actors within the Antarctic Treaty System as strategists, generalists, and specialists. The revealed policy advantage (RPA) captures the relative specialization of each actor in specific policy domains, while the excess KL divergence quantifies deviations from structural equilibrium, shedding light on latent interests not reflected in formal submissions. Together, these metrics enable the identification of hidden dynamics and latent priorities, enriching our understanding of the Antartic Treaty System beyond its surface-level commitments to science and innovation.

These results challenge the broad characterization of the Antarctic Treaty System as merely a hub for scientific collaboration. Instead, they reveal a structured network of interrelated topics where countries navigate governance through strategic submissions. By analyzing revealed policy advantages and excess KL divergence, we uncover how countries prioritize policy domains, expose potential latent interests, and identify imbalances in engagement. This approach provides a clearer understanding of the dynamics shaping the Antarctic governance landscape and offers a basis for refining future analyses of international treaty systems.

To construct the space of concerns, we analyze the working papers and information documents submitted to the Antartic Treaty System from 1959 to 2023. The data is collected from the official Antartic Treaty System website and includes all working papers and information documents submitted by treaty members during this period. We focus on the topics covered in these documents, which are categorized into different policy domains such as resource management, territorial claims, scientific research, and environmental protection.

For these documents, a clustering was performed based on the co-submission patterns of working papers and information documents. The clustering algorithm groups topics that are frequently submitted together by the same agents. The Leiden algorithm was then used to identify clusters of agents that share similar policy interests as indicated by their co-submission patterns \cite{Traag2019}.

The co-submission patterns suggest that actors who frequently align on particular topics also tend to maintain consistent collaboration across a wider range of issues. Although nearly all parties engage with the Antarctic Treaty System to some degree, their levels of involvement and patterns of interaction differ substantially.

A particularly active cluster (dark blue) stands out for its sustained engagement across virtually all topics. This group includes the UK, Norway, the US, Australia, New Zealand, and Argentina—nations with long-standing interests in Antarctica and historically strong involvement in its governance. Their interconnections are dense, reflecting tight coordination and repeated co-submissions on a wide array of issues.

A second, also active cluster (green) features countries such as Germany, Belgium, the Netherlands, Poland, and South Korea. While these actors contribute extensively, their collaborative structure is more diffuse: they are broadly interconnected, but without the tightly knit substructures seen in the dark blue group. Their engagement spans many topics, often in coordination with different partners across the cluster.

A third cluster (red) appears more regionally anchored and includes China, India, and several South American countries such as Ecuador and Colombia. These actors are increasingly active and tend to collaborate among themselves, though their submission patterns are somewhat more focused and less evenly distributed across all topics. Their presence suggests growing engagement with the Antarctic governance regime, possibly reflecting expanding national programs or geopolitical interests.

Another notable group (yellow) consists primarily of observer organizations and specialized entities, such as SCAR (Scientific Committee on Antarctic Research), COMNAP (Council of Managers of National Antarctic Programs), and the United Nations bodies. This group plays a crucial role in supporting scientific, logistical, and legal frameworks within the Treaty System. Their contributions are specialized but influential, often underpinning the technical basis for policy discussions.

Finally, there is a smaller and more loosely affiliated set of actors (brown), including several newer or less active consultative parties. Their participation is more selective, with contributions that are often limited to specific areas of expertise or interest. These actors tend to collaborate opportunistically, aligning with other groups on an issue-by-issue basis.

\subsection{Sensitivity Analysis}
To evaluate the stability of the observed structure, we performed a sensitivity analysis by removing each country from the space of concerns and computing the root mean squared error between the proximity matrix of the full space and the space without that country. The process is repeated by iteratively removing the agent that produces the maximal difference between the current set of countries and the set of countries without that agent. This greedy approach would help us understand what each agent contributes to the construction of the space of concerns in Antartica.

In Figure \ref{fig:sensitivity-analysis}, we observe that the space of concerns is robust against the removal of a large fraction of countries. This is evidenced by the relatively gradual increase in spectral distance observed through the first 60\% of removals, suggesting that the global structure of the space is maintained even when many individual contributors are excluded. This robustness implies a degree of redundancy or modularity in how countries co-structure the space, where no single actor—apart from a few key exceptions—critically determines the overall topology.

However, we also observe distinct inflection points in the curve, where the removal of specific countries results in sharp increases in spectral distance. Notably, countries such as China, Germany, France, and the United Kingdom contribute disproportionately to the coherence of the product space. Their removal induces substantial distortions in the spectral representation, indicating their role as structural anchors or bridges across different regions of the space. In contrast, even countries with large economic output, such as the United States and India, show a more subdued effect on the global geometry, underscoring that structural importance is not merely a function of scale but also of positionality within the network.

Toward the final stages of the removal sequence, the spectral distance rapidly approaches its maximum, signifying a collapse of the original structure. Interestingly, some of the countries responsible for this final increase—such as Sweden, Venezuela, and Romania—are not necessarily central by economic metrics but appear to occupy topologically unique or peripheral positions that preserve fragile clusters or minor components.

Overall, the analysis highlights the asymmetric role of countries in shaping the global structure of the space of concerns. While many actors contribute incrementally, a small subset exerts an outsized influence on the cohesion and organization of the space. This spectral sensitivity analysis thus offers a principled approach for identifying structurally pivotal agents beyond conventional economic indicators.

\subsection{Statements on Antartica}
Each member in the Antartic Treaty System has a unique set of interests and priorities that shape their engagement with the region. The following sections provide an overview of the intentions and activities of each Consultative Party, as well as those that have signed the treaty but do not currently meet the requirements for consultative status. The information is based on official statements, national Antarctic programs, and publicly available data. The statements were collected on 03-07-2025 and are subject to change as countries update their policies and activities in Antarctica.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Argentina}: An original signatory and Consultative Party. Has a long history of presence. Intention focuses on scientific research, environmental protection, logistics, and asserting sovereignty claims (suspended by the Treaty). Activities include maintaining multiple year-round and seasonal stations (Esperanza, Marambio, Carlini, etc.), conducting extensive research programs, and providing logistical support. [Source: Dirección Nacional del Antártico (DNA)]
    \item \textbf{Australia}: An original signatory and Consultative Party. Intention focuses on scientific research, environmental stewardship, preserving Antarctica as a natural reserve, and asserting sovereignty claims (suspended by the Treaty). Activities include managing the Australian Antarctic Program (AAP), operating year-round stations (Casey, Davis, Mawson) and a subantarctic station (Macquarie Island), conducting research, and operating research/supply vessels. [Source: Australian Antarctic Division (AAD)]
    \item \textbf{Belgium}: An original signatory and Consultative Party. Intention is primarily scientific research and demonstrating environmental best practices. Activities include operating the Princess Elisabeth Antarctica station, known for its zero-emission design, and conducting research in various scientific disciplines. [Source: Belgian Polar Secretariat]

\subsubsection{Organizations (Relevant to Antarctica):}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{ACAP} (Agreement on the Conservation of Albatrosses and Petrels): Its intention is the conservation of albatrosses and petrels. Many listed species breed or forage in the Antarctic and sub-Antarctic regions, making the agreement highly relevant to activities within the Antarctic Treaty Area, particularly fisheries interactions. [Source: ACAP official website]

    \item \textbf{ASOC} (Antarctic and Southern Ocean Coalition): An international environmental NGO. Its intention is to protect the Antarctic environment and its associated ecosystems. Activities involve monitoring meetings under the Antarctic Treaty System, advocating for stronger environmental protection measures, and raising public awareness. [Source: ASOC official website]

    \item \textbf{ATS} (Antarctic Treaty System): This is the framework itself, not an organization with an official statement of itself. Its "intention" is embodied in the Antarctic Treaty (1959) and subsequent agreements (like the Protocol on Environmental Protection - Madrid Protocol). The core intentions are to ensure Antarctica is used exclusively for peaceful purposes, promote scientific investigation, and ensure freedom of scientific investigation and exchange of information. [Source: Antarctic Treaty Secretariat]

    \item \textbf{CCAMLR} (Commission for the Conservation of Antarctic Marine Living Resources): An international commission established by a convention forming part of the ATS. Its intention is the conservation of Antarctic marine living resources, while allowing for rational harvesting. Activities involve setting fishing quotas, establishing protected areas, and monitoring ecosystems in the Southern Ocean. [Source: CCAMLR official website]

    \item \textbf{COMNAP} (Council of Managers of National Antarctic Programs): An association of national Antarctic programs. Its intention is to provide a forum for exchanging information and best practices in Antarctic logistics and operations, facilitating international cooperation in supporting scientific research. [Source: COMNAP official website]

    \item \textbf{IAATO} (International Association of Antarctica Tour Operators): An industry organization. Its intention is to advocate and promote safe and environmentally responsible tourism in Antarctica. Activities involve developing and implementing operational guidelines, collecting data, and working with Treaty Parties on tourism management. [Source: IAATO official website]

    \item \textbf{IGP} Clubs (International Group of P\&I Clubs): An association of mutual marine liability insurers. Their relevance to Antarctica is indirect; they provide liability coverage for ships operating globally, which includes vessels engaged in research, tourism, or fishing in Antarctic waters. Their "intent" in this context is to ensure their members comply with relevant regulations (like the Polar Code) and handle potential claims (e.g., pollution, injury) if incidents occur in the region. They don't have operational intent in Antarctica itself. [Source: IGP\&I website - check sections on Polar Code or specific risks]

    \item \textbf{IHO} (International Hydrographic Organization): An intergovernmental organization for charting and navigation. Its intention is to ensure the world's seas, oceans, and navigable waters are surveyed and charted. Activities relevant to Antarctica include coordinating hydrographic surveys in the Southern Ocean and contributing to international bathymetric charting efforts (like GEBCO/IBCSO). [Source: IHO website - look for Southern Ocean / Polar regions work]

    \item \textbf{IMO} (International Maritime Organization): A UN specialized agency for maritime safety and security and prevention of marine pollution from ships. Its intention is to develop international regulations for shipping. Key activity relevant to Antarctica is the development and enforcement of the Polar Code, which governs ship operations in polar waters, including safety and environmental protection requirements. [Source: IMO website - Polar Code]

    \item \textbf{IOC} (Intergovernmental Oceanographic Commission - UNESCO): Promotes international cooperation and coordinates programs in marine research, services, observation systems, hazard mitigation and capacity development. Its intention relevant to Antarctica involves coordinating ocean observation systems in the Southern Ocean, supporting marine research, and contributing to climate science related to the region. [Source: IOC-UNESCO website - Southern Ocean activities]

    \item \textbf{IOPC} Funds (International Oil Pollution Compensation Funds): Provide financial compensation for oil pollution damage caused by spills from oil tankers. Their relevance to Antarctica is contingent on an oil pollution incident occurring in Convention waters involving a contributing ship. They don't have standing operational intent in Antarctica, but a role in financial response to incidents. [Source: IOPC Funds website]

    \item \textbf{IPCC} (Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change): A UN body that assesses the science related to climate change. Its intention is to provide comprehensive assessment reports. Activities relevant to Antarctica include assessing the scientific literature on climate change impacts in polar regions, including ice sheet melt, sea level rise, and ecosystem changes, which are covered in dedicated chapters or sections of their reports. It does not conduct operational activities in Antarctica. [Source: IPCC website - Assessment Reports, Working Group reports on Cryosphere/Ocean]

    \item \textbf{IPY-IPO} (International Polar Year - International Programme Office): The International Polar Year was a major international research program that took place in 2007-2008 (and a predecessor in 1882-83, 1932-33, 1957-58). The IPO was the office that helped coordinate the 2007-2008 IPY. It was a time-limited initiative. The "intention" was to stimulate and coordinate a burst of international, interdisciplinary polar research. The IPO no longer exists as an active coordinating body for a current IPY, though its legacy and data remain. [Source: Information about past IPY programs]

    \item \textbf{IUCN} (International Union for Conservation of Nature): A global authority on the status of the natural world and the measures needed to safeguard it. Its intention is to influence, encourage and assist societies to conserve nature. Its work is relevant to Antarctica through assessments of polar species (e.g., via the Red List), policy recommendations on marine protected areas, climate change impacts, and overall conservation of the Southern Ocean ecosystem. [Source: IUCN website - search for Antarctica, Southern Ocean, Polar]

    \item \textbf{SCAR} (Scientific Committee on Antarctic Research): An interdisciplinary body of the International Science Council (ISC). Its intention is to initiate, develop and coordinate high quality international scientific research in the Antarctic region, and on the role of the Antarctic region in the Earth system. It provides objective scientific advice to the Antarctic Treaty System. [Source: SCAR official website]

    \item \textbf{UNEP} (United Nations Environment Programme): The leading global environmental authority. Its intention is to provide leadership and encourage partnership in caring for the environment. Its work is relevant to Antarctica through monitoring environmental issues, assessing climate change impacts, promoting conservation of polar biodiversity, and collaborating with other bodies on environmental governance relevant to the region. [Source: UNEP website - search for polar, Antarctica, Southern Ocean]

    \item \textbf{WMO} (World Meteorological Organization): A UN specialized agency for meteorology (weather and climate), operational hydrology and related geophysical sciences. Its intention is to facilitate worldwide cooperation. Activities relevant to Antarctica include coordinating meteorological observations from stations and satellites, facilitating data exchange, supporting climate research and monitoring in the region. [Source: WMO website - Polar activities]

    \item \textbf{WTO} (World Trade Organization): Deals with the rules of trade between nations. Its relevance to Antarctica is minimal and indirect, potentially related to trade in fish products from the Southern Ocean (managed under CCAMLR) or services like tourism. It does not have an official statement of intention or activities in Antarctica itself.

    \item \textbf{Korea (DPRK - North Korea)}: There is no public information suggesting North Korea has any official program, stated intention, or activities in Antarctica. They are not a party to the Antarctic Treaty.
\end{itemize}
