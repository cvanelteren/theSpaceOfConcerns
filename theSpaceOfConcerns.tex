\begin{abstract}
    Consensus institutions often mask strategic competition because formal unanimity obscures disagreement in official outcomes. We address this by inferring actor-level activity fingerprints from revealed topic priorities, rather than from collaboration ties alone. Using economic-complexity tools on six decades of Antarctic Treaty submissions, we build a ``space of concerns'' in which policy topics are linked when actors recurrently co-specialize in them. We find that members often bundle low-salience scientific topics with adjacent contentious ones, signaling intent through composition rather than volume. Archetypal analysis identifies three modes—Specialists, Moderate Specialists, and Aligned Generalists—and the regime moves from early volatility to a structured, self-reinforcing ecosystem. As participation broadens, structural alignment tightens, challenging the notion that complexity drifts toward entropy. The space recovers the latent topology of diplomatic engagement and offers a portable blueprint for decoding strategic evolution in consensus-based governance.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Consensus regimes are often treated as low-conflict settings because formal unanimity masks disagreement in official outcomes. Yet in practice, contestation is displaced into agenda-setting, linkage, and selective engagement \cite{Stone2013,Abbott2015,Hurd2015,Gardiner2025,Drubel2023HiddenContestation}. The core empirical problem is therefore methodological: how do we detect strategic behavior when formal records appear procedural and voting signals are absent?

We address this by shifting attention from collaboration ties to revealed issue activity. Rather than inferring strategy from who collaborates with whom, we extract each actor's activity fingerprint from the topics they repeatedly prioritize in the forum, their concerns. We then ask: which topics are related and what concern space do they form, (how) does it evolve, and (how) do actors move through it under consensus constraints?

The Antarctic Treaty System (ATS) is an ideal case for this question. Since 1959, the ATS has demilitarized a resource-rich continent while suspending sovereignty claims \cite{AntarcticTreaty1959,NTI2024}, and it is frequently described as exceptional and technocratic \cite{BASTreatyExplained,Joyner1998,Dodds2019,Yao2021}. Yet strategic pressures around militarization and resource access persist and often surface indirectly through science, logistics, and compliance files \cite{DoddsBoulegue2023,Brady2017,Haward2012,Szpak2025}. What enters (or stays off) the ATS agenda is therefore a critical site of strategic maneuver.

Our approach adapts economic complexity tools to governance \cite{Hidalgo2007,Orsini2019}. Using revealed policy advantage, we link topics that members jointly specialize in to build a \emph{space of concerns}, a co-specialization map where distance reflects shared engagement, not semantics. This map makes consensus politics empirically legible in three ways. First, it shows how the ATS tends to channel historically sensitive matters (demilitarization, extraction, tourism access) through a procedural-environmental core rather than direct confrontation. Second, it supports temporal analysis: hazard models reveal that new attention tends to move locally within the space, indicating structured, path-dependent agenda shifts. Third, it enables strategic profiling: archetype analysis identifies recurring engagement modes (Specialists, Moderate Specialists, Aligned Generalists) and their trajectories.

Substantively, we find a dense procedural–environmental hub with distinct arms toward extraction/science, tourism/visitation, and coordination, and we show that participation growth coincides with tighter structural alignment rather than fragmentation. Methodologically, the space of concerns offers a portable way to study how consensus regimes filter conflict into patterned issue bundles—useful for monitoring resilience, anticipating pressure points, and comparing across consensus-based institutions beyond Antarctica.

\subsection{The Geometry of Diplomatic Attention}
The space of concerns reveals a dense procedural--environmental core—opening statements, exchange of information, environmental monitoring/reporting, liability, inspections, educational issues—from which distinct arms extend toward extraction, tourism/visitation, and coordination topics \cref{fig:the_space_of_concerns}. Edges connect topics that any member co-specializes in (RCA $>$ 1 in both), so proximity captures shared engagement rather than semantic similarity.

This geometry encodes two defining constraints of the ATS. First, the 1959 Antarctic Treaty demilitarized the continent, pushing strategic maneuver into procedure and science \cite{AntarcticTreaty1959,Joyner1998,Yao2021}. Second, the 1991 Madrid Protocol banned mineral resource activities while permitting tightly regulated scientific drilling \cite{MadridProtocol1991,Koivurova2005}. Accordingly, a resource/science arm runs from management plans through environmental domains analysis and marine acoustics to drilling and sub-glacial lakes. We interpret this branch as a dual-use governance zone: the same technical capacities that support legitimate science can also carry latent strategic value, so they remain close enough to the procedural core to be monitored and negotiated \cite{Sampaio2022,Aus2025,DoddsBoulegue2023,Szpak2025}. This interpretation does not imply covert military use in each instance; it indicates that technical capabilities with potential strategic spillovers are institutionally managed as sensitive. Bioprospecting sits nearer the core than the extraction tip, consistent with its framing as science with potential biomedical value rather than classic extraction.

Inspections sit on the spine between the core and the extraction/conservation arms, reflecting their Article VII role as the compliance hinge linking procedural debate to operational practice. Marine living resources and marine protected areas connect that spine to a tourism/visitation arm (tourism and NGO activities, site guidelines), showing how access is routed via conservation and reporting obligations rather than overt bargaining \cite{Haward2012,CCAMLRConsensus2022}. A coordination/operations arm carries CEP strategy discussions, general operations, and educational issues, marking softer coordination and legitimacy work. Climate change occupies a spur between the core and extraction, indicating recognition without full absorption into the procedural bundle. The map therefore replaces a technocratic blur with a structured agenda in which sensitive issues are typically translated into procedural and environmental forms before they can advance \cite{Sampaio2022}.

Two natural questions follow. First, how do interests evolve through this issue space over time? Second, does this geometry correspond to strategic movement by actors, or is it merely descriptive? We address the first by decomposing the space of concerns over time. In \cref{fig:space_of_ribbons} we visualize shifting attention by drawing flows between topics that remain salient (RCA $>$ 1) across adjacent periods. The space starts with a relatively small set of topics focused on cooperation (exchange of information, science issues, cooperation with other organizations) and accountability (inspections). As the system matures, the space grows and diversifies (\cref{fig:space_of_ribbons}, bottom inset). The procedural core shows increased attention to liability in the 1980s, with renewed focus in the 2000s. Educational issues rise in the 1980s and persist across periods. A brief focus on historical sites also emerges from management concerns in the preceding decade. Overall, the core operates as a recurrent hub: attention in the 1980s shifts from the core toward extraction-adjacent topics such as marine living resources, bioprospecting, and drilling, while climate-related concern in the same period feeds into marine protected areas.

The second question is answered by the hazard analysis: despite high volatility in which topics are active, new attention is biased toward nearby issues in the space rather than random jumps (\cref{fig:hazard-distance-timeseries}). This indicates that the space is an empirically grounded aggregate of governance behavior, not just a descriptive visualization. It also implies that the map can be used to trace strategic trajectories, including how actors expand, cluster, and reposition their portfolios, which motivates the next section.

Having mapped the thematic landscape—the space of concerns—we next examine how actors navigate it. We use archetype analysis to identify recurrent engagement modes over time, allowing actor trajectories to be interpreted as movement along a common strategic frontier rather than as disconnected case studies. This step matters because consensus institutions reward strategic composition of portfolios: actors rarely contest rules directly, but they do vary in where and when they allocate attention.

Our analysis identifies three strategic archetypes: Specialist, Moderate Specialist, and Aligned Generalist. \Cref{fig:archetype_profiles} shows that these modes separate cleanly on diversity and specialization metrics: Specialists concentrate attention on narrow portfolios, Aligned Generalists sustain broad portfolios that track system structure, and Moderate Specialists occupy an intermediate region. Robustness diagnostics in Appendix \cref{fig:archetype_robustness} support this specification. RSS drops from 92.12 at $k=2$ to 3.57 at $k=3$, then changes only marginally and non-monotonically for higher $k$, consistent with an elbow at three modes. Jackknife perturbations remain moderate (L2 shifts 0.007--0.380), and feature sensitivity indicates that KL contributes most strongly to the dominant axes, while entropy mainly reshapes positions nonlinearly toward highly specialized cases without overturning the three-mode structure. Substantively, the three modes capture distinct approaches to influence: concentrated intervention, broad procedural alignment, and hybrid positioning.

The simplex embedding reveals concrete strategic positions. The Aligned Generalist corner is anchored by long-running core actors such as the United Kingdom and Australia, while many issue-bounded organizations and small-footprint actors cluster toward the Specialist corner (e.g., IPY-IPO, WTO, ICAO). Moderate Specialist positions are populated by actors combining broad participation with selective emphasis (e.g., Ukraine, Finland, France, South Africa). This distribution is consistent with the institutional structure of the ATS: established consultative actors can sustain broad engagement across procedural and operational files, while narrower actors tend to exert influence through targeted agenda windows.

These modes are not merely descriptive; they are associated with differential timing performance. \Cref{fig:pioneer_scatter} shows a strong nonlinear relation between breadth and early-adoption residuals ($R^2=0.52$): early movers appear both among broad portfolio actors (United Kingdom, United States, Australia, Chile) and among narrow but highly active actors (e.g., IMO), while many mid-to-broad participants enter later than expected. This pattern indicates two viable pathways to early influence under consensus constraints: broad alignment with procedural-core dynamics, or concentrated pushes in narrow opportunity windows. Consistent with this, early-adoption association is positive for Aligned Generalists ($r=0.256$), negative for Specialists ($r=-0.225$), and weakly positive for Moderate Specialists ($r=0.151$; Appendix \cref{fig:archetype_robustness}). Empirically, intermediate strategies more often sit closer to the trend line or below it than either extreme. As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:residuals_gdp}, this variation is not explained by GDP per capita alone.

Consensus can hide strategy. In forums where unanimity suppresses overt conflict signals, strategic competition is displaced into issue selection, linkage, and timing \cite{Stone2013,Hurd2015,Abbott2015,Drubel2023HiddenContestation}. The space of concerns makes that displaced competition observable by tracking revealed activity across topics, rather than relying on formal voting outcomes or collaboration ties alone.

This contribution complements, rather than replaces, existing social-science approaches. Legal-institutional analyses explain mandate design and norm development; diplomatic-history work explains sequencing and practice; co-sponsorship networks explain who aligns with whom \cite{Keohane1984,Dodds2019,Yao2021,Sampaio2022}. Our added value is different: we recover where actors place attention in the issue topology, how that topology is structured, and how actors move through it over time.

Substantively, the ATS appears as a structured system with a procedural--environmental core that absorbs and refracts sensitive agendas. The core is not merely administrative. It functions as a governance filter through which high-friction issues are translated into inspectable, reportable, and negotiable forms. This is most visible in the extraction/science arm, where technical files sit adjacent to politically sensitive resource and security questions. That pattern is consistent with current scholarship on dual-use pressures in Antarctica and the persistence of geopolitical tension under a formally cooperative regime \cite{Brady2017,DoddsBoulegue2023,Aus2025,Szpak2025}.

The temporal results strengthen this interpretation. New attention does not diffuse randomly; it tends to move locally in the concern space, and actor trajectories cluster into a small number of recurrent engagement modes. This indicates structured adaptation rather than drift. In practical terms, change in the ATS is more likely to appear as recombination of adjacent concerns than as abrupt displacement to distant issues. The archetype diagnostics do not prove a unique truth at $k=3$, but they do support a stable and policy-interpretable three-mode summary for the present data.

These findings also sharpen how consensus stability should be interpreted. Relative institutional stability does not imply absence of strategic pressure; it can reflect effective filtering of disagreement into recurring issue bundles \cite{CCAMLRConsensus2022,Gardiner2025}. Influence in such systems is therefore expressed through portfolio positioning and timing of entry into gateway concerns, not only through activity volume or economic size.

For policy practice, the map can be used as an early-warning layer. As pressures intensify around tourism, climate adaptation, logistics, and infrastructure, likely contention points should emerge first near existing high-salience clusters, especially around inspections, reporting, and management instruments that connect multiple branches of the system \cite{Haward2012,Gardiner2025}. More broadly, this strategy can be applied to other consensus-based institutions where formal agreement conceals strategic differentiation \cite{Vabulas2013,Abbott2015}: it provides a tractable way to monitor where pressure accumulates and how institutions adapt before conflict becomes explicit.

\section{Methods}
\subsection{Data}
We analyze working papers and information documents submitted to the Antarctic Treaty System (ATS) from 1959 to 2023. The corpus was compiled from the official ATS document archive and enriched with thematic labels, submitting parties, and document types \cite{AntarcticTreatyHandbook,Yaryab2024}. Documents submitted by the ATS Secretariat were retained for completeness.

Data collection was performed on 6 February 2026 from the ATS website (\url{https://www.ats.aq/devAS/Meetings/DocDatabase?lang=e}) using the antarctic-database-go collector by Carlo Hamalainen (\url{https://github.com/carlohamalainen/antarctic-database-go}). A key property of this framework is reproducibility: it stores cached HTTP responses and local copies of retrieved source documents, records processing status in SQLite, and supports deterministic re-runs and auditability of the extraction pipeline. That means that if the website changes or documents are removed, the collected dataset can be reconstructed from the cached responses, ensuring that the analysis can be replicated even if the source data evolves.

\subsection{Constructing the Space of Concerns}
Let $x(a,i)$ denote the number of submissions by agent $a$ on topic $i$. We define the normalized activity share
\begin{equation}\label{eq:activity_share}
\pi(a,i) = \frac{x(a,i)}{\sum_j x(a,j)},
\end{equation}

and the system-wide importance of topic $i$ as

\begin{equation}\label{eq:topic_importance}
Z_i = \frac{\sum_c x(c,i)}{\sum_{c,j} x(c,j)}.
\end{equation}

The Revealed Policy Advantage (RPA) is then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rpa}
    \rho(a,i) = \frac{\pi(a,i)}{Z_i}.
\end{equation}

Topics are linked by proximity
\begin{equation}\label{eq:proximity}
\begin{aligned}
\phi(i,j)
&= \min \Bigl\{\\
    &P\bigl(\rho(c,i)\ge 1 \mid \rho(c,j)\ge 1\bigr),\\
    &P\bigl(\rho(c,j)\ge 1 \mid \rho(c,i)\ge 1\bigr)\\
\Bigr\}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
yielding a weighted topic network.
We construct both an aggregate space (for the regime-wide map) and time-resolved spaces by rebuilding proximities within each time window, allowing the topology itself to evolve rather than assuming a fixed structure over time.
This adapts the product-space proximity logic from economic complexity to governance concerns \cite{Hidalgo2007}.


\subsection{Pioneering Behavior and Strategic Group Analysis}
To move beyond static measures of engagement and capture the temporal dynamics of strategy, we define an early-adoption residual designed to quantify how proactive each actor is, conditional on its breadth of specialization. The calculation proceeds in four steps.

First, we establish a topic emergence year for each policy topic. This is defined as the first year in which the cumulative number of submissions on that topic reaches 15\% of its total historical volume. This metric identifies when a topic transitions from a niche issue to part of the mainstream agenda.

Second, we determine a country entry year for each country-topic pair. Using a 5-year rolling window to smooth out short-term fluctuations, we calculate the Revealed Comparative Advantage (RCA) for every country in every topic for each window. A country is considered to have ``entered'' a topic in the first year its RCA value is greater than or equal to 1.0, signifying specialization.

Third, we calculate a relative pioneer index. For each country-topic specialization, we compute a ``relative lag'': the difference between the country's entry year and the topic's emergence year (or the country's first year of activity, whichever is later). A negative lag indicates pioneering behavior, i.e. adopting a topic before it becomes mainstream, while a positive lag indicates a follower. The pioneer index for a given country is the negative of its average relative lag across all topics it specializes in, such that a higher, positive score corresponds to earlier adoption.

Finally, we define the strategic groups using the early-adoption residual. A quadratic curve was fitted to the data of pioneer index versus engagement breadth (number of specialized topics) to model the system's main behavioral trend. We then calculated the residual for each actor (their vertical distance from this curve). Actors were categorized into three groups based on the tertiles of the residual distribution: the top third were labeled over-performers, the bottom third under-performers, and the middle third on-trend. This method allows for a robust, distribution-agnostic classification of actors who significantly deviate from the expected timing given their breadth.

\subsection{Latent Interest Diffusion}

Latent interests are inferred via diffusion:
\[
f^{(t+1)}_a = \alpha \hat{\theta}_a + (1-\alpha) D^{-1} W f^{(t)}_a,
\]
where $W$ is the proximity matrix and $\alpha$ controls retention of revealed signal. We report results integrated over $\alpha$ (averaging across a grid), so inference does not hinge on a single diffusion setting.

Specialism is quantified as excess KL divergence relative to a one-hot baseline diffusion.

\subsection{Inferring Strategic Behavior Regimes using Archetype Analysis}
\subsubsection{Time-Varying Archetype Analysis}
To characterize the evolution of extreme states within the system, we employed Time-Varying Archetype Analysis (AA). Unlike centroid-based clustering (e.g., K-means) which identifies average profiles, Archetype Analysis identifies ``pure types'' or extremes located on the convex hull of the data distribution \cite{cutler1994archetypal}. The aim is to find extreme dimensions that represent idealized modes of engagement, allowing us to interpret strategic behavior in terms of archetypal profiles rather than average tendencies. This is particularly relevant in the context of the Antarctic Treaty System, where strategic behavior may manifest as distinct modes. It is therefore expected that members will show mixtures of these modes, rather than clustering around a single average profile.

\subsubsection{Mathematical Formulation}
Archetype Analysis performs a constrained matrix factorization of a data matrix $X \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times m}$, where $n$ is the number of observations and $m$ is the number of metrics. For a given number of archetypes $k$, the algorithm seeks to find a set of archetypes $Z \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times m}$ governed by two primary constraints:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Each observation is a convex combination of the archetypes: $X \approx AZ$, where $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times k}$ is the matrix of mixing coefficients.
    \item Each archetype is a convex combination of the actual data points: $Z = BX$, where $B \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times n}$ defines the archetypes as weighted averages of observations.
\end{enumerate}

The optimization objective is to minimize the Residual Sum of Squares (RSS):
\begin{equation}
    \min_{A, B} \| X - ABX \|^2_F
\end{equation}
subject to the constraints:
\begin{equation}
    a_{ij} \ge 0, \sum_{j=1}^k a_{ij} = 1 \quad \text{and} \quad b_{ji} \ge 0, \sum_{i=1}^n b_{ji} = 1
\end{equation}
This formulation ensures that the archetypes are physically interpretable as idealized extremes of the observed population.

\subsubsection{Feature Standardization and Scaling}
To ensure that the archetype geometry was not biased by disparate feature scales (e.g., entropy in bits vs. excess metrics), we applied Z-score standardization. Features were scaled globally across the entire temporal extent of the dataset to maintain a consistent coordinate system:
\begin{equation}
    x'_{i} = \frac{x_i - \mu_{global}}{\sigma_{global}}
\end{equation}
This global scaling allows for the tracking of absolute movements in the ``frontier'' of the data over time, rather than just relative changes within a single year.

\subsubsection{Temporal Rolling Windows and Longitudinal Stability}
To capture the dynamic evolution of these extremes, archetypes were fitted using a sliding temporal window of $W = 5$ years. For each year $t$, the algorithm was trained on the subset of data points $X_t$ falling within the interval $[t - \lfloor W/2 \rfloor, t + \lfloor W/2 \rfloor]$.

To resolve the ``label switching'' problem inherent in iterative matrix factorization, we implemented a longitudinal tracking mechanism. Archetypes in window $t$ were matched to those in window $t-1$ by minimizing the total Euclidean distance between archetype coordinates. This assignment was solved using the Kuhn-Munkres (Hungarian) algorithm:
\begin{equation}
    \min \sum_{i=1}^k d(z_{i, t-1}, z_{j, t})
\end{equation}
where $d(\cdot)$ is the Euclidean distance in the feature space. This ensures that archetype identities (e.g., ``the high-diversity extreme'') remain consistent throughout the trajectory, enabling a stable longitudinal analysis.

\subsection{Interpreting Latent Interests and Methodological Limits}
Concern-space proximities capture observed co-specialization patterns; they can reflect procedural bundling or path-dependent collaboration, not only intrinsic topical similarity. The diffusion operator smooths revealed profiles into their structural neighbourhoods and should be read as a projection, not a behavioural model of negotiation. Excess Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence flags misalignment between revealed and structure-implied profiles but does not diagnose intent—capacity constraints, domestic priorities, or self-restraint can yield similar gaps. KL asymmetry and sensitivity to low probabilities are handled with smoothing and a one-hot baseline, giving a conservative lower bound on divergence expected from pure specialization. Quantitative diagnostics should therefore be paired with qualitative knowledge of mandates and capabilities.

Diffusion is structural rather than causal; Specialism signals concentration or misalignment, not deception; and ``latent'' interest refers to structure-implied engagement, not unobserved preferences. Similarly, behavioral regime classification captures temporal patterns in trajectories but does not directly infer strategic intent—volatile behavior may reflect responsiveness, capacity constraints, or institutional role rather than deliberate exploration. These caveats bound the interpretive claims of the analysis.

\subsection{Sensitivity Analysis}
To evaluate the stability of the observed structure, we performed a sensitivity analysis by removing each country from the space of concerns and computing the root mean squared error between the proximity matrix of the full space and the space without that country. The process is repeated by iteratively removing the country that produces the maximal difference between the current set of countries and the set of countries without that country. This greedy approach helps us understand what each country contributes to the construction of the space of concerns in Antarctica.

In \cref{fig:sensitivity-analysis}, we observe that the space of concerns is robust against the removal of a large fraction of countries. This is evidenced by the relatively gradual increase in spectral distance observed through the first 60\% of removals, suggesting that the global structure of the space is maintained even when many individual contributors are excluded. This robustness implies a degree of redundancy or modularity in how countries co-structure the space, where no single actor—apart from a few key exceptions—critically determines the overall topology.

However, we also observe distinct inflection points in the curve, where the removal of specific countries results in sharp increases in spectral distance. Notably, countries such as China, Germany, France, and the United Kingdom contribute disproportionately to the coherence of the product space. Their removal induces substantial distortions in the spectral representation, indicating their role as structural anchors or bridges across different regions of the space. In contrast, even countries with large economic output, such as the United States and India, show a more subdued effect on the global geometry, underscoring that structural importance is not merely a function of scale but also of positionality within the network.

Toward the final stages of the removal sequence, the spectral distance rapidly approaches its maximum, signifying a collapse of the original structure. Interestingly, some of the countries responsible for this final increase—such as Sweden, Venezuela, and Romania—are not necessarily central by economic metrics but appear to occupy topologically unique or peripheral positions that preserve fragile clusters or minor components.

Overall, the analysis highlights the asymmetric role of countries in shaping the global structure of the space of concerns. While many actors contribute incrementally, a small subset exerts an outsized influence on the cohesion and organization of the space. This spectral sensitivity analysis thus offers a principled approach for identifying structurally pivotal actors beyond conventional economic indicators.

Mapping actors onto this terrain (\cref{fig:actors_on_space}) shows clustered, asymmetric contributions. Panels A/B (aggregate) reveal four Leiden communities \cite{Traag2019}: a dominant blue core of longstanding consultative parties with permanent stations and logistics (e.g., United States, United Kingdom, Australia) that spans governance anchors and operations (blue dominates most topic bars); a red community mixing organizations (IPY-IPO, IUCN) with mid-activity or newer programs (e.g., Turkey, Bulgaria, Czech Republic, Malaysia) that takes the largest share on cooperation-with-other-organizations and adjacent governance items; a purple group anchored by smaller, education-focused programs (e.g., Peru, Uruguay) centered on the educational-issues callout; and a green cluster led by science-intensive programs (China, India, Russia) that targets a narrow science/operations niche around sub-glacial lakes and environmental domains analysis.

Panel C embeds these clusters into the space of concerns, marking each cluster’s highest relative-interest topics (RCA $\geq 1$) with dashed circles, and panel D tracks how dominance shifts over time. We can coarsely delineate between four engagement modes based on where each cluster concentrates its influence.

Backbone cluster (blue). The blue cluster maintains broad dominance across governance and operations, with only a modest dip in the 1990s as new entrants expand participation. Foundational topics—marine acoustics, management plans, inspections, and opening statements—remain blue-led throughout, indicating that early signatories anchor the procedural spine even as participation broadens.

Coordination cluster (red). The red community surges in the early 2000s around cooperation with other organizations, planning, and status reports, coinciding with the International Polar Year and tourism guidelines. The mix of time-bounded coordination bodies (IPY-IPO) and smaller or newer national programs aligns with this timing: their influence concentrates when cross-institutional coordination becomes salient, then tapers as those agenda items recede.

Educational/procedural cluster (purple). The purple group peaks in the late 1990s/early 2000s on educational and adjacent procedural issues. Many of these actors run seasonal or small-footprint programs (e.g., Uruguay’s Artigas base, Peru’s Machu Picchu station), which aligns with a strategy of targeted visibility through education and outreach rather than broad operational control.

Science/extraction cluster (green). The green cluster gains traction in the 2010s on science and resource-extraction niches—drilling, sub-glacial lakes, biological prospecting—aligning with the expansion of large national programs (China’s multi-station network, India’s Maitri/Bharati, Russia’s long-standing multi-station presence). Its appearances are sharp and bounded, reflecting targeted pushes into specific opportunity zones rather than broad agenda control.

While the blue cluster is dominant, other clusters show a greater intensity in interest in specific clusters, indicating that influence is both concentrated and distributed depending on the topic. The regime thus exhibits a layered structure with a procedural core held by longstanding actors, and specialized branches where newer or niche programs can assert influence through targeted engagement.

\subsection*{Archetype robustness}
We validate the archetype specification along five dimensions (Figure \ref{fig:archetype_robustness}). (A) Bootstrap stability: refitting on resampled rows keeps each archetype concentrated around its baseline location, indicating that the geometry is not a sampling artifact. (B) Actor jackknife: removing each of the five most-active actors changes coordinates only moderately (L2 shifts range 0.007--0.380), indicating that no single major contributor determines the structure. (C) Model order: reconstruction error falls rapidly from 92.12 ($k=2$) to 3.57 ($k=3$), then changes only marginally and non-monotonically for higher $k$, consistent with an elbow at three archetypes rather than unrestricted complexity growth. (D) Feature sensitivity: alternative feature specifications (entropy variant, standardized features, and KL-only) move archetypes but preserve separation of strategic extremes. The largest shifts occur under KL-only (mean shift 8.51, max 10.30), indicating that KL defines the dominant axis of variation, while entropy contributes nonlinear refinement that is most visible among highly specialized actors. (E) Transition structure: dominant-mode transitions are strongly diagonal (89\%), showing persistence in strategic orientation with limited cross-mode switching. (F) External association: archetype mixtures correlate with early-adoption residuals (Specialist $r=-0.225$, Moderate Specialist $r=0.151$, Aligned Generalist $r=0.256$), providing criterion validity for the strategy labels.

Taken together, these checks do not prove uniqueness of $k=3$, but they make the three-mode interpretation substantially more credible for the present dataset and inferential goal.
