\begin{abstract}
    Consensus institutions can hide strategic differentiation because formal unanimity obscures disagreement in official outcomes. Using six decades of Antarctic Treaty submissions, we infer actor-level activity fingerprints from revealed topic priorities and build a ``space of concerns'' in which institution-defined topics are linked when actors recurrently co-specialize in them. The resulting topology supports a minimum adequate three-regime summary. Actors frequently span adjacent regimes but effectively never bridge the two extremes directly without passing through the middle. Portfolio change is local and path dependent: adoption odds decline with concern-space distance, regime shifts are mostly within or adjacent, and early agenda entry is associated with both broader portfolios and stronger anchoring within one regime rather than with membership in any single regime. Among strongly anchored actors, some regime combinations---especially pairings between a narrower procedural-information position and a broader frontier-protection position---are more complementary than redundant, even though the system retains a substantial shared core. A simple retain-and-adopt mechanism, conditioned on the observed active actors, budgets, and pooled concern space, closely reproduces mean breadth, topic popularity, and local entry patterns. The ATS therefore appears neither uniformly governed nor cleanly partitioned, but as a shared arena with structured positions, local movement, and selective complementarity.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Consensus regimes are often described through their formal decision rules, but unanimity in outcomes does not by itself reveal how actors position themselves across issues. In such settings, much of the observable record is procedural rather than electoral: there are few voting traces, but there are repeated choices about what to submit, emphasize, and revisit. Existing ATS work explains institutions, legal design, diplomacy, and co-sponsorship, but it does not recover the issue topology through which actors repeatedly position themselves. The core empirical problem is therefore methodological: how do we recover differentiated patterns of engagement when formal records appear unanimous and overt conflict is muted \cite{Stone2013,Abbott2015,Hurd2015,Gardiner2025,Drubel2023HiddenContestation}?

We address this by shifting attention from collaboration ties to revealed issue activity. This paper sits at the intersection of ATS scholarship and a broader literature that uses document collections to recover structure in complex policy systems. Our contribution is not simply to apply text-analytic or topic-based tools to Antarctica, but to reconstruct a relational space of concerns from repeated patterns of actor specialization. Rather than inferring positioning only from who collaborates with whom, we extract each actor's activity fingerprint from the \emph{institution's own topic categories} that they repeatedly prioritize in the forum. We then ask three connected questions: what relational issue topology emerges from co-specialization in those categories, how stable is that topology over time, and how do actors move through it under consensus constraints?

The Antarctic Treaty System (ATS) is a useful case for this question. Antarctica has no permanent population, but it carries scientific value, strategic significance, and long-standing resource potential. The ATS emerged in 1959 to demilitarize the continent and suspend sovereignty conflict, and it later broadened into a governance arena for science, environmental protection, logistics, tourism, and resource-related questions \cite{AntarcticTreaty1959,NTI2024,BASTreatyExplained,Joyner1998,Dodds2019,Yao2021}. It is also unusually well suited to computational analysis: it is consensus-based, historically stable, and leaves behind a long documentary record in which submission volume rises sharply after the 1990s and concentrates in a subset of recurring issue categories (Appendix \cref{fig:topic-submission-overview}). In the ATS, these submissions are not incidental paperwork; they are the main medium through which actors place issues on the agenda, frame concerns, and seek collective attention. What enters, persists on, or falls away from the ATS agenda therefore offers a direct observational window into how a formally unanimous institution organizes attention over time \cite{Guterres2023}.

Our approach adapts economic complexity tools to governance \cite{Hidalgo2007,Orsini2019}. Using revealed policy advantage, we link \emph{institution-defined} topics that members jointly specialize in to build a \emph{space of concerns}, a co-specialization map where distance reflects shared engagement in ATS categories rather than linguistic similarity. Because the topics come from the institution's own taxonomy, the resulting geometry reflects patterned participation within the ATS itself.

This construction makes the institutional record legible in three ways. First, it reveals a dense procedural-environmental core alongside more specialized but still connected regions around extraction/science, tourism/visitation, and coordination. Second, it supports temporal analysis: new attention tends to move locally within the space, indicating structured, path-dependent agenda shifts rather than broad diffusion across the full topology. Third, it enables actor profiling: regime position and regime anchoring reveal recurring engagement modes and how actors time entry from those positions.

Substantively, we find a dense procedural--environmental hub with distinct arms toward extraction/science, tourism/visitation, and coordination, and we show that participation growth coincides with tighter structural alignment rather than fragmentation. This also speaks to a broader literature on the growth and differentiation of international bodies: institutional development is not only a matter of accumulating mandates or members, but also of reorganizing attention within a shared issue arena. More broadly, the paper speaks to a general problem of consensus governance: how formally unanimous institutions can still organize differentiated patterns of attention and positioning beneath shared outcomes. Methodologically, the space of concerns offers one way to study how consensus institutions organize attention into shared but differentiated issue topologies, with identifiable boundary zones that can be compared across cases beyond Antarctica.

\subsection{The Geometry of Diplomatic Attention}
The space of concerns in \cref{fig:the_space_of_concerns} is a numerically constructed proximity network based on co-specialization: edges link topic pairs for which at least one member specializes in both (RPA $>$ 1 in each), so distance reflects recurrent co-engagement rather than semantic similarity. For exposition, we use qualitative labels (e.g., ``core'', ``corridor'', ``chain'') to describe visually dense or elongated regions of this network; these labels are interpretive and are not themselves model-estimated clusters.

Under this interpretive reading, a dense procedural--environmental zone (opening statements, exchange of information, environmental monitoring/reporting, environmental protection, liability, inspections) sits near the center, with thinner extensions toward resource/science frontiers, tourism/visitation, and operations/planning.

This geometry is consistent with two defining ATS constraints. The 1959 Antarctic Treaty demilitarized the continent, channeling strategic contestation into procedure and science \cite{AntarcticTreaty1959,Joyner1998,Yao2021}, while the 1991 Madrid Protocol prohibited mineral resource activities but allowed tightly regulated scientific drilling \cite{MadridProtocol1991,Koivurova2005}. In the map, this appears as a resource/science corridor linking management and assessment topics to environmental domains analysis, marine acoustics, drilling, and sub-glacial lakes. We interpret this corridor as a dual-use governance zone: capabilities with legitimate scientific value but potential strategic spillovers remain institutionally tethered to core procedural nodes where they can be monitored and negotiated \cite{Sampaio2022,Aus2025,DoddsBoulegue2023,Szpak2025}. Bioprospecting sits closer to core procedural topics than to the corridor's extraction-end topics (especially Drilling and Sub glacial Lakes), which fits this logic.

Bridge topics structure movement between core and periphery. Inspections function as a compliance hinge, while Human Footprint and wilderness values and Marine Protected Areas connect the core to an access-oriented chain (tourism and NGO activities, prevention of marine pollution, site guidelines for visitors, and search and rescue), indicating that access pressures are mediated through conservation and safety obligations rather than overt distributive bargaining \cite{Haward2012,CCAMLRConsensus2022}. Climate change and CEP strategy appear as adjacent connectors rather than detached outliers. In the full concern space, the topology is connected but bounded: only 37 of 990 topic pairs have zero proximity (3.74\%), no topic is fully isolated, and the zero-proximity ties are concentrated in a small set of boundary topics, especially Sub glacial Lakes, State of the Antarctic Environment Report SAER, Environmental Domains Analysis, Drilling, Marine Protected Areas, and Prevention of marine pollution (Appendix \cref{fig:heatmap_concerns}). A full ribbon view of all actor-topic supports with RPA $> 1$ across adjacent periods is shown in Appendix \cref{fig:space-ribbon-full}; ribbon width reflects weighted country support rather than static edge magnitude. Simple network summaries point in the same direction: density and giant-component size increase as participation expands, while clustering and betweenness remain low, indicating a broadly connected space rather than tightening local cliques or single-topic brokerage; a greedy member-removal sensitivity check likewise remains modest until late in the deletion sequence, suggesting that the geometry is not driven by any single actor (Appendix \cref{fig:space_network_metrics,fig:sensitivity-analysis}).



\subsection{Modes of Engagement and Actor Strategies}
The Space of Concerns reveals how members allocate attention across issues and how they position themselves in ATS governance. As shown in \cref{fig:portfolio_space_ridgelines}, attention is uneven: countries concentrate in distinct parts of the issue space, and those clusters define three broad engagement regimes. Those regimes come from a contiguous weighted partition of the ordered topic axis: we smooth aggregate excess engagement along the 1D ordering, iteratively fit three weighted centers, snap those centers to nearby topics, and define final regime boundaries at the midpoints between adjacent centers. Member colors in panel A then follow the dominant regime, defined by the largest share of each member's excess portfolio mass in that final three-region partition. We treat three regimes as the minimum adequate summary of the ordered concern space rather than as an arbitrary clustering choice (Appendix \cref{fig:regime-k-validation}). Importantly, the 1D MDS axis is an embedding for visualization rather than a uniform metric ruler: local neighborhood structure is preserved approximately, but dense portions of the network appear compressed while sparse portions are expanded. These regime-position summaries focus on the 52 actors with at least three RPA-specialized topics in the aggregate archive; actors with one or two specialized topics remain in the corpus and enter other analyses where relevant, but such sparse portfolios do not support stable centroid, spread, or fuzzy-occupancy summaries.

Taken together, the regimes form a gradient rather than isolated silos. Regime 1 (institutional coordination and information exchange) emphasizes opening statements, science and education, information exchange, and ATS reporting and operational topics. Regime 2 (compliance and environmental management) centers on environmental protection, EIA, liability, waste and pollution, legal-institutional matters, inspections, and protected-area governance. Regime 3 (frontier impacts, resources, and strategic planning) concentrates on drilling, mineral resources, tourism pressure, SAER/environmental domains, marine acoustics, sub-glacial lakes, CEP strategy, and multiyear planning, where scientific capability, environmental risk, and long-horizon positioning intersect. The full topic-to-regime assignment is reported in Appendix Table~\ref{tab:regime_topics_full}.

These regimes capture dominant orientations, not fixed blocs. Members most strongly aligned with Regime 1 include Switzerland, Malaysia, Estonia, and Bulgaria; with Regime 2, UNEP, COMNAP, Belarus, and IUCN; and with Regime 3, the Russian Federation, the United States, New Zealand, and Australia (Appendix Table~\ref{tab:regime_alignment_full}). Some countries remain tightly concentrated in one region, while others maintain mixed portfolios across neighboring regions, separating structural position from strategic flexibility as agendas shift.

Australia illustrates this distinction: although its portfolio spans all three regions, its center of gravity remains in Regime 3 (share $=0.553$), indicating a diversified but not genuinely between-regime position (Appendix Table~\ref{tab:regime_alignment_full}).

A fuzzy-threshold view of this regime-summary subset confirms that these are adjacent occupancy zones rather than rigid classes: actors commonly span Regime 1+2 or Regime 2+3, only one actor spans all three regions at the chosen threshold, and no actor spans Regime 1+3 without also passing through Regime 2 (Appendix \cref{fig:fuzzy-regime-classes}). The regimes are also historically ordered in the archive itself. Regime 1 actors are later and shorter-tenure on average, Regime 2 actors are intermediate, and Regime 3 actors are the earliest and longest-running participants (Appendix \cref{fig:regime-tenure}).

These positions overlap with, but are not reducible to, collaboration or broad geopolitical grouping. Co-sponsorship communities concentrate unevenly across the same concern regions, and country pairs that co-submit more often also show greater portfolio overlap, but simple geodesic proximity is only weakly associated with portfolio overlap and does not recover the same structure well; EU/NATO pairings coded using current rather than year-specific historical bloc memberships are not unusually similar, while BRICS alignment is positive but small-sample and therefore exploratory (Appendix \cref{fig:actors_on_space,fig:portfolio-share-collaboration,fig:geo-portfolio-overlap,fig:bloc-overlap}). We therefore treat the bloc appendix as a coarse present-day grouping check rather than a time-aligned historical test. At the same time, the regime structure is not only redundant. Among strongly anchored sovereign actors with at least five specialized topics, hard Regime 1--3 pairs cover more distinct topics and show less internal overlap than size-matched baselines, while hard 2--2 pairs look comparatively redundant. A fuzzy-class check that separates adjacent bridge actors preserves the main complementary pattern: pure 1--3 and 1+2--3 pairings remain the clearest complementary combinations, whereas 1+2--2 pairings are more redundant (Appendix \cref{fig:fuzzy-pair-coverage,fig:regime-pair-institutional}).

\subsection{Growth of Interest Portfolios is not Random Drift but Local Movement in the Concern Space}
The regime map above is descriptive; the next question is whether movement through this space is structured. It is. In the pooled specification, adoption odds fall as concern-space distance increases ($\beta_{\mathrm{distance}}=-0.634$, odds ratio $=0.53$). The empirical adoption curve in \cref{fig:hazard-distance-timeseries} shows the same pattern nonparametrically, with adoption highest in the nearest distance bins and falling to near zero in the farthest bins. The sign remains negative across alternative space constructions, and the full-history aggregate variant is slightly steeper than the instantaneous and cumulative-lagged variants (aggregate: $-0.652$; instantaneous: $-0.539$; cumulative-lagged: $-0.533$; Appendix \cref{fig:hazard-space-sensitivity}). The same figure shows that topic engagement is sticky once adopted: retention remains high across years under both strict and gap-tolerant definitions, indicating that ATS portfolios are not rebuilt from scratch at each meeting. Consistent with this locality, dominant-regime assignments are highly persistent in rolling 5-year windows among actor-windows with at least three specialized topics: 82.0\% of member transitions remain in the same regime, 99.0\% remain in the same or an adjacent regime, and direct Regime 1$\leftrightarrow$Regime 3 jumps are rare (1.0\%). A direct 1-year versus 5-year comparison (Appendix \cref{fig:regime-window-sensitivity}) shows the expected smoothing: same-regime persistence rises from 58.6\% to 82.0\%, while direct Regime 1$\leftrightarrow$Regime 3 jumps fall from 2.7\% to 1.0\%. Together, these results support interpreting engagement modes as path-dependent orientations rather than static labels, because actors tend to extend portfolios into nearby concerns rather than making long-range jumps.

\subsection{Local Entry and Selective Persistence Recover ATS Portfolio Structure}

The path-dependence results establish local movement, but they do not show whether that mechanism is strong enough to recover the observed organization of ATS portfolios. We therefore fit a simple retain-and-adopt model that holds fixed the observed active actor set in each year, the actor-specific budgets they deploy, and a pooled concern-space geometry, then asks whether portfolio dynamics alone can reproduce the observed system. The model updates portfolios in three steps: actors retain part of their prior support, adopt new topics locally in the concern space, and allocate effort within that selected support. Despite these restrictions, the model reproduces the observed ATS closely: it matches mean actor breadth (observed $3.35$, simulated $3.32$), mean topic popularity (observed $2.45$, simulated $2.45$), annual variation in breadth ($r=0.87$), annual variation in topic popularity ($r=0.98$), and local entry rank in $\phi$-space (observed $0.677$, simulated $0.679$; \cref{fig:split-support-model}). Across 64 repeated simulations with the fitted parameters held fixed, the 5--95\% process band remains centered on the observed series and the retain-and-adopt model's local-entry rank stays in a narrow interval around the observed value ($0.662$--$0.688$ versus $0.677$; \cref{fig:split-support-model}). Approximate parameter intervals remain clearly positive for allocation persistence, local fit, entry locality, and retention on prior portfolio weight (Appendix \cref{fig:split-support-params}). Repeated year-balanced thinning of the raw corpus leaves this pattern intact: at 40--80\% retention of submissions, breadth-tracking correlations remain between $0.78$ and $0.86$, and local-entry rank stays within $0.016$ of the observed value (Appendix \cref{fig:split-support-thinning}). This is a mechanism check rather than a full theory of ATS evolution, since participation, budget formation, and possible changes in the concern space are held outside the model. The substantive implication is that much of the observed ATS can be generated by selective persistence, local entry, and constrained allocation, rather than by smooth diffusion across the full issue topology.

\subsubsection{Agenda Pioneering Reflects Breadth and Regime Anchoring}
Early agenda entry is not concentrated in one dominant regime. As shown in \cref{fig:pioneer_plot}, the 64 actors with a defined pioneer index include pioneers from all three regimes, but earlier movers tend to be both broader and more strongly concentrated within one regime rather than evenly mixed across the full space. A simple OLS using the relative pioneer index as the outcome shows that both breadth ($\beta_{\mathrm{breadth}}=0.26$, $p=0.001$) and max regime share ($\beta_{\mathrm{anchor}}=17.70$, $p<0.001$) are positive predictors of earlier entry; dominant-regime indicators are not distinguishable from zero. This pattern is stable to alternative cumulative-volume thresholds for defining topic emergence (Appendix \cref{fig:emergence-threshold-sensitivity}). Pioneering therefore appears less as membership in one privileged bloc than as regime-positioned timing from a clear portfolio base.

\section{Discussion}
Consensus regimes are often treated as low-conflict settings, as formal unanimity in official outcomes obscures underlying disagreement. In practice, however, contestation is displaced into agenda-setting, issue linkage, and selective engagement, allowing actors to shape collective trajectories without overtly contesting decisions on record \cite{Stone2013,Hurd2015,Abbott2015,Drubel2023HiddenContestation}. The space of concerns makes that displaced competition observable by tracking revealed activity across topics, rather than relying on formal voting outcomes or collaboration ties alone.

This contribution complements, rather than replaces, existing social-science approaches. Legal-institutional analyses explain mandate design and norm development; diplomatic-history work explains sequencing and practice; co-sponsorship networks explain who aligns with whom \cite{Keohane1984,Dodds2019,Yao2021,Sampaio2022}. Our added value is different: we recover where actors place attention in the issue topology, how that topology is structured, and how actors move through it over time.

Substantively, the ATS appears as a structured concern space organized around a dense procedural--environmental hub, with more specialized but still connected regions around extraction and science, tourism and visitation, and coordination. This topology does not imply a detached extraction arena; rather, it is consistent with an institutional setting in which historically contentious issues, especially resource extraction, are tightly constrained and therefore remain embedded in adjacent procedural, scientific, and environmental pathways. That reading is consistent with current scholarship on dual-use pressures in Antarctica and the persistence of geopolitical tension under a formally cooperative regime \cite{Brady2017,DoddsBoulegue2023,Aus2025,Szpak2025}.

The space of concerns should nevertheless be read as a relational summary of joint specialization, not as an external semantic ontology of Antarctic issues. Topics are nearby because actors repeatedly place disproportionate attention on them together, so the geometry reflects both strategic behavior and the institution's own classificatory structure. The dense core may therefore capture both substantive centrality and the fact that many contested issues are processed through common procedural and environmental channels. Likewise, the pooled space is best understood as a stable long-run summary rather than as a claim that the latent issue geometry is literally fixed over time; Appendix \cref{fig:decadal-space-stack} shows the same fixed layout populated decade by decade, illustrating that occupation and edge intensity shift even when a common topology remains analytically useful.

The temporal results strengthen this interpretation. New attention does not diffuse randomly; it tends to move locally in the concern space, and actor trajectories cluster into a small number of recurrent engagement regimes. This indicates structured adaptation rather than drift. In practical terms, change in the ATS is more likely to appear as recombination of adjacent concerns than as abrupt displacement to distant issues. The regime diagnostics do not imply hard blocs, but they do support a stable and policy-interpretable three-regime summary for the present data. The fuzzy occupancy check sharpens this point: actors frequently bridge adjacent regimes, but effectively never span Regime 1 and Regime 3 directly without also occupying Regime 2. The mechanism check reported in the Results is consistent with this reading: ATS portfolios are better described by selective persistence and local expansion within a shared issue arena than by smooth diffusion across the full topology. In that sense, ATS development appears not simply as growth in volume or scope, but as the emergence of a more ordered and differentiated structure of attention.

The same regime geometry also clarifies what this paper does and does not mean by division of labor. The ATS does not look like a fully partitioned system in which actors simply split the agenda into non-overlapping domains. The shared procedural--environmental core remains substantial. But neither is the system merely redundant. Once actors are conditioned on strong regime anchoring, some pairings become more complementary than others. In particular, the clearest complementarity appears across the Regime 1--3 boundary: those pairs cover more distinct topics and overlap less than expected, and the effect survives a fuzzy-class sensitivity check that separates pure anchors from adjacent bridge actors. The accompanying institutional profile suggests that these complementary pairs often bridge the ATS hierarchy itself. They are more likely than expected to combine a Consultative Party, which participates in formal consensus decision-making, with a Non-Consultative Party, which can participate in the system but does not hold the same decision rights, and they are also more likely to link gateway states, which have privileged logistical access to the region, to other actors. They are also more often claimant-linked than expected, but the claimant enrichment should be read together with tenure: the effect is largely carried by long-established Regime 3 anchors rather than by claimant status alone (Appendix \cref{fig:fuzzy-pair-coverage,fig:regime-pair-institutional,fig:regime-tenure}). This is therefore better understood as selective complementarity inside a shared arena than as a global division-of-labor equilibrium.

At the same time, the retain-and-adopt model should be read at the level it was designed to address. It captures average breadth, average topic popularity, local entry, and period-to-period stability better than it captures the exact realized actor-topic support in any one period. That limitation is substantively useful rather than merely technical. It suggests that this paper identifies the broad geometry of ATS attention and the main local growth rules, but not yet the finer-grained forces that determine why actors occupying the same formal consensus regime still choose different issue bundles and timings. Future work can look more closely at the mechanisms that shift portfolios over time within that shared concern space.

These findings also sharpen how consensus stability should be interpreted. Relative institutional stability does not imply absence of strategic pressure; it can reflect effective filtering of disagreement into recurrent issue pathways with stable boundary zones \cite{CCAMLRConsensus2022,Gardiner2025}. Influence in such systems is therefore expressed through portfolio positioning and timing of entry into gateway concerns, not only through activity volume or economic size.

For policy practice, the map can be used as an early-warning layer. As pressures intensify around tourism, climate adaptation, logistics, and infrastructure, likely contention points should emerge first near existing high-salience clusters, especially around inspections, reporting, and management instruments that connect multiple branches of the system \cite{Haward2012,Gardiner2025}. More broadly, this strategy suggests a tractable way to examine other consensus-based institutions where formal agreement conceals strategic differentiation \cite{Vabulas2013,Abbott2015}, though that extension remains to be demonstrated directly.

\section{Methods and Data}
\subsection{Data Description and Processing}
We analyze working papers and information documents submitted to the Antarctic Treaty System (ATS) from 1961 to 2025. The corpus was compiled from the official ATS document archive and enriched with thematic labels, submitting parties, and document types \cite{AntarcticTreatyHandbook,Yaryab2024}. Documents submitted by the ATS Secretariat were retained for completeness.

Data collection was performed on 6 February 2026 from the ATS website (\url{https://www.ats.aq/devAS/Meetings/DocDatabase?lang=e}) using the antarctic-database-go collector by Carlo Hamalainen (\url{https://github.com/carlohamalainen/antarctic-database-go}). A key property of this framework is reproducibility: it stores cached HTTP responses and local copies of retrieved source documents, records processing status in SQLite, and supports deterministic re-runs and auditability of the extraction pipeline. That means that if the website changes or documents are removed, the collected dataset can be reconstructed from the cached responses, ensuring that the analysis can be replicated even if the source data evolves.

The category labels are Secretariat-assigned (not NLP-derived) and thus the space reflects co-participation within the ATS’s own issue taxonomy rather than semantic similarity. Because agenda items vary across meetings over time, the Secretariat created cross-temporal categories to provide a harmonized, consistent classification of documents across the archive (ATS correspondence). For preprocessing, we expanded multi-party entries into one row per submitting actor and deduplicated attachment-linked records using unique \texttt{paper\_id}, yielding 6,591 unique papers in the 1961--2025 analysis panel. We also excluded records labeled \texttt{ALL} or \texttt{Other}, which functioned as placeholder categories and introduced noise into topic assignment (especially in recent years). Our unit of analysis is the \emph{submitting actor}, not legal status within the Treaty. We therefore retain consultative parties, non-consultative parties, observers, expert bodies, and Secretariat submissions whenever they appear in the document archive. The reason is substantive: this paper studies issue positioning and agenda formation in the ATS document system, not formal voting power at the final consensus stage. These actor types are not institutionally equivalent, but they all contribute to what enters the agenda, how issues are framed, and which concerns are repeatedly linked in practice. Mixing them would be inappropriate for a model of formal decision authority; it is appropriate for a model of revealed issue activity in the broader ATS arena \cite{Sampaio2022,AntarcticTreatyHandbook}.


\subsection{Constructing the Space of Concerns}
Let $x(a,i)$ denote the number of submissions by actor $a$ on topic $i$, where actors include consultative parties, non-consultative parties, observers, relevant organizations, and the ATS Secretariat whenever they appear as submitters in the archive. We define the normalized activity share
\begin{equation}\label{eq:activity_share}
\pi(a,i) = \frac{x(a,i)}{\sum_j x(a,j)},
\end{equation}

and the system-wide importance of topic $i$ as

\begin{equation}\label{eq:topic_importance}
Z_i = \frac{\sum_c x(c,i)}{\sum_{d,j} x(d,j)}.
\end{equation}

The Revealed Policy Advantage (RPA; an RCA-style specialization index) is then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rpa}
    \rho(a,i) = \frac{\pi(a,i)}{Z_i}.
\end{equation}

Topics are linked by proximity
\begin{equation}\label{eq:proximity}
\phi(i,j)
=
\min_a\!\left\{
\begin{aligned}
    &\Pr\!\bigl(\rho(a,i) > 1 \mid \rho(a,j) > 1\bigr),\\
    &\Pr\!\bigl(\rho(a,j) > 1 \mid \rho(a,i) > 1\bigr)
\end{aligned}
\right\}.
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}
        \Pr(\rho(a,j)>1 \mid \rho(a,i)>1) =\frac{\sum_a \mathbf{1}[\rho(a,i)>1 \land \rho(a,j)>1]}
        {\sum_a \mathbf{1}[\rho(a,i)>1]},
\end{equation}
yielding a weighted topic network. Taking the minimum of the two conditional probabilities serves three purposes. First, using conditional rather than joint probability controls for differences in topic prevalence: two relatively rare topics can still register as close if specialization in one reliably coincides with specialization in the other, whereas a joint probability would mechanically shrink with rarity. Second, taking the minimum prevents one-way dependence from overstating proximity. If a niche topic almost always appears alongside a broad topic, ($P(\text{broad}\mid \text{niche})$) may be high even when the reverse is low; the minimum forces the proximity to be limited by that weaker direction. Third, this yields a symmetric measure of relatedness, so the link between topics reflects mutual co-specialization rather than directional inclusion. Probabilities are estimated empirically across members within the relevant analysis window.
We construct both an aggregate space (for the regime-wide map) and time-resolved spaces by rebuilding proximities within each time window, allowing the topology itself to evolve rather than assuming a fixed structure over time.
This adapts the product-space proximity logic from economic complexity to governance concerns \cite{Hidalgo2007}.

\subsection{Constructing Regimes from the Concern Space}
The three engagement regimes in the main text are not obtained by clustering actors directly. Instead, they are defined as contiguous regions of the recovered topic space and actors are then assigned by how much of their excess portfolio mass falls into each region. This choice is deliberate: the substantive object of interest is movement and positioning \emph{within} a shared concern space, so the regimes should respect that geometry rather than grouping actors by similarity alone.

We begin from the full actor-topic specialization matrix, not from an already aggregated topic profile. The aggregate proximity matrix $\phi$ in \cref{eq:proximity} is first constructed from repeated co-specialization across actors, so actor-level differences enter the regime procedure initially through the concern-space geometry itself. We then convert $\phi$ into a weighted distance graph by assigning each positive edge the length
\begin{equation}
    d(i,j) = -\log\!\bigl(\max\{\phi(i,j), \varepsilon\}\bigr),
\end{equation}
with a small $\varepsilon>0$ to avoid taking logs of zero. We then compute all-pairs shortest-path distances on this topic graph and obtain a one-dimensional coordinate for each topic by classical multidimensional scaling (MDS). The resulting axis is used only as an ordered embedding: it provides a consistent left-to-right representation of the concern space, but it is not interpreted as a literal Euclidean policy spectrum.

To identify broad regions on this ordered space, we aggregate excess specialization across actors using
\begin{equation}
    g_i = \sum_a \max\{\rho(a,i)-1,0\},
\end{equation}
where $g_i$ is large when many actors place disproportionate attention on topic $i$. This aggregate signal is introduced only \emph{after} the concern-space axis has been constructed. The regime fit itself is then performed on topic positions in the 1D embedding, not on actor observations directly. In the weighted specification used in the paper, we smooth $g_i$ over the 1D ordering with a short Gaussian kernel and locate $R=3$ region centers by an iterative 1D assignment-and-update step. Starting from three evenly spaced initial centers, each topic is assigned to its nearest center in the 1D embedding and each center is updated to the \emph{weighted} mean position of the topics currently assigned to it, using the smoothed excess-engagement signal as the weight. After convergence, each center is snapped to the nearest observed topic, and the regime boundaries are taken as the midpoints between adjacent centers. An unweighted comparison therefore corresponds to the same 1D procedure with equal topic weights: the concern-space geometry remains actor-derived, but the partition itself is no longer pulled toward high-engagement parts of that space.

This procedure yields three contiguous topic blocks on the ordered concern space. The regimes therefore summarize broad zones of issue concentration rather than unrestricted network communities. They are intentionally more structured than off-the-shelf actor clustering methods such as $k$-means, hierarchical clustering, or Leiden community detection, which can be useful descriptively but do not enforce the spatial contiguity needed for our interpretation of local movement and regime transitions. It is therefore useful to distinguish the two objects involved: the concern space itself is a topology of joint interest, whereas the weighted regime partition is an engagement summary of how actors occupy that topology. We selected $R=3$ as the minimum adequate partition rather than as a universal optimum. Appendix \cref{fig:regime-k-validation} reports two complementary checks using the same snapped weighted-center rule as the main analysis. First, weighted within-region inertia shows a clear elbow at $k=3$. Second, compressing actor portfolios to $k$-regime shares yields a large gain in preservation of pairwise actor similarity from $k=2$ to $k=3$, while additional regimes provide only modest improvement and rapidly create very small residual regions. We therefore treat three regimes as the smallest partition that captures the main portfolio geometry without over-fragmenting the concern space.

Actors are then positioned relative to this fixed topic partition. For actor $a$, let $r(i)\in\{1,2,3\}$ denote the regime assigned to topic $i$. We compute regime weights from the same excess specialization signal,
\begin{equation}
    \omega_{ar}
    =
    \frac{\sum_{i:r(i)=r} \max\{\rho(a,i)-1,0\}}
         {\sum_i \max\{\rho(a,i)-1,0\}},
\end{equation}
so that $\omega_{ar}$ is the share of actor $a$'s excess portfolio mass falling in regime $r$. The actor's dominant regime is the maximizer of $\omega_{ar}$, and the dominant-regime share $\max_r \omega_{ar}$ measures regime anchoring. This is the quantity used in the main-text discussion of strong aligners, mixed portfolios, and regime-persistence transitions. The complete topic-to-regime mapping is reported in Appendix Table~\ref{tab:regime_topics_full}.

For descriptive regime-position summaries (Figure~2, Appendix Table~\ref{tab:regime_alignment_full}, \cref{fig:fuzzy-regime-classes,fig:regime-tenure}), we retain actors with at least three specialized topics in the aggregate archive. For regime-transition matrices, we apply the analogous per-window requirement of at least three specialized topics before assigning a dominant regime. These filters avoid forcing centroid- and spread-based regime summaries onto one- or two-topic portfolios.


\subsection{Hazard Model for Local Topic Adoption}
To test whether agenda expansion is local in the concern space, we estimate a rolling member-level hazard model on adjacent annual windows. For each member $a$, topic $i$, and transition $t-1 \rightarrow t$, an observation is included only if the topic is at risk:
\[
\text{at-risk}_{a i t}=1 \iff \rho_{a i, t-1} < 1.
\]
The binary outcome is then
\[
y_{a i t}=1 \iff \rho_{a i, t} \ge 1 \ \text{and}\ \rho_{a i, t-1} < 1.
\]
Within each member-window panel we fit a logistic model with standardized covariates
\begin{dmath}
\Pr(y_{a i t}=1)=\mathrm{logit}^{-1}\!\left(
\beta_0
\!+\!\beta_d\,\tilde d_{a i, t-1}
\!+\!\beta_b\,\widetilde{\mathrm{breadth}}_{a,t-1}
\!+\!\beta_p\,\widetilde{\mathrm{pop}}_{i,t-1}
\right),
\end{dmath}
where $d_{a i, t-1}$ is the minimum concern-space distance from topic $i$ to the member's previously active topics, $\mathrm{breadth}_{a,t-1}$ is the number of previously active topics, and $\mathrm{pop}_{i,t-1}$ is prior topic popularity (share of members with $\rho \ge 1$). The plotted heatmap reports $\beta_d$ by member and window; negative values indicate preferential expansion into nearby topics. We summarize each window with the median and interquartile range across members.
In the main specification, distances are computed from the aggregate concern space; Appendix \cref{fig:hazard-space-sensitivity} reports sensitivity to instantaneous and cumulative-lagged spaces.
For regime-persistence diagnostics, we assign each member-window a dominant regime from region shares computed on $\max(\rho-1,0)$ over the fixed three-region topic partition used in \cref{fig:portfolio_space_ridgelines}, exclude member-windows with fewer than three specialized topics, and then tabulate member transitions across adjacent windows. We report same-regime, adjacent-regime, and far-jump ($1\leftrightarrow3$) transition rates.

\subsection{Sequential Retain-and-Adopt Model of Portfolio Dynamics}
To test whether the ATS can be approximated by a simple generative mechanism, we fit a reduced-form model conditioned on three observed objects: (i) the active actor set in each year, (ii) actor-specific budgets $K_{at}$, and (iii) a fixed pooled concern-space matrix $\phi$ from \cref{eq:proximity}. The goal is not to explain who becomes active or how budgets arise, but whether the observed organization of interests can be reproduced once those constraints are given.

A single-rule support equation proved too blunt because it conflated topic retention and topic entry. We therefore model portfolio change sequentially. Let $x_{ait}$ denote actor $a$'s submission mass on topic $i$ at time $t$, let $x_{at}$ denote the corresponding topic-allocation vector, and let $K_{at}=\sum_i x_{ait}$ be the observed total budget in that year. Define the support indicator $z_{ait}=1[x_{ait}>0]$ and the support set $S_{at}=\{i:z_{ait}=1\}$. We write $s_{ai,t-1}=x_{ai,t-1}/K_{a,t-1}$ for the prior topic share and $p_{i,t-1}$ for prior topic popularity. The latent Bernoulli draws $z^{\mathrm{ret}}_{ait}$ and $z^{\mathrm{ent}}_{ait}$ indicate whether a previously held topic is retained and whether a previously absent topic is entered, respectively; $\alpha_i^{\mathrm{ret}}$, $\alpha_i^{\mathrm{ent}}$, and $\alpha_i$ are topic-specific intercepts, while $\delta_{\mathrm{ret}}$ and $\delta_{\mathrm{ent}}$ are stage-specific intercept shifts. For topics already in the portfolio, retention is
\begin{dmath}
\Pr\!\left(z^{\mathrm{ret}}_{ait}=1 \mid z_{ai,t-1}=1\right)
=
\mathrm{logit}^{-1}\!\Bigl(
\alpha_i^{\mathrm{ret}}
\!+\!
\delta_{\mathrm{ret}}
\!+\!
\lambda_{\mathrm{ret}} s_{ai,t-1}
\!+\!
\gamma_{\mathrm{ret}} p_{i,t-1}
\Bigr).
\end{dmath}
For topics not yet held, entry is
\begin{dmath}
\Pr\!\left(z^{\mathrm{ent}}_{ait}=1 \mid z_{ai,t-1}=0\right)
=
\mathrm{logit}^{-1}\!\Bigl(
\alpha_i^{\mathrm{ent}}
\!+\!
\delta_{\mathrm{ent}}
\!+\!
\beta_{\mathrm{ent}}\,\mathrm{Fit}_{ait}
\!+\!
\gamma_{\mathrm{ent}} p_{i,t-1}
\Bigr),
\end{dmath}
with
\begin{dmath}
\mathrm{Fit}_{ait}
=
\frac{1}{|S_{a,t-1}|}
\sum_{j\in S_{a,t-1}}
\phi(i,j),
\end{dmath}
so new topics are favored when they are close to the actor's prior support in the native concern space. The same $\mathrm{Fit}_{ait}$ term enters the allocation stage below as the average $\phi$-proximity of candidate topic $i$ to the actor's previous support.

After combining retained and entered topics into the support set $S_{at}$, the observed budget $K_{at}$ is allocated across the selected support by
\begin{dmath}
x_{at} \sim \mathrm{Multinomial}\!\Bigl(K_{at}, \mathrm{softmax}\!\Bigl( \alpha_i \!+\! \rho s_{ai,t-1} \!+\! \beta\,\mathrm{Fit}_{ait} \!+\! \gamma p_{i,t-1}\Bigr)_{i\in S_{at}} \Bigr).
\end{dmath}
Topic intercepts $\alpha_i$, $\alpha_i^{\mathrm{ent}}$, and $\alpha_i^{\mathrm{ret}}$ are empirical baselines estimated from pooled topic mass, pooled entry frequency, and pooled retention frequency. The free coefficients $(\rho,\beta,\gamma)$, $(\delta_{\mathrm{ent}},\beta_{\mathrm{ent}},\gamma_{\mathrm{ent}})$, and $(\delta_{\mathrm{ret}},\lambda_{\mathrm{ret}},\gamma_{\mathrm{ret}})$ are estimated by maximum likelihood. A final scalar intercept calibration is then applied to the entry and retention logits during simulation to align support levels while keeping the fitted slopes fixed.

This sequential retain-and-adopt model performs well despite its simplicity. The fitted allocation stage remains strongly persistent and local ($\rho=4.29$, $\beta=3.97$), the entry stage is also local ($\beta_{\mathrm{ent}}=0.58$), and retention favors previously important topics ($\lambda_{\mathrm{ret}}=0.42$). After the intercept calibration, the model reproduces mean actor breadth (observed $3.35$, simulated $3.32$), mean topic popularity (observed $2.45$, simulated $2.45$), annual variation in breadth ($r=0.87$), annual variation in topic popularity ($r=0.98$), and local entry rank in $\phi$-space (observed $0.677$, simulated $0.679$). The main implication is that a large share of ATS portfolio structure can be generated by selective persistence, local entry, and constrained allocation, without requiring actors to diffuse uniformly across the issue space.

Approximate parameter intervals tell the same story. In the retain-and-adopt model, allocation persistence and local fit remain clearly positive ($\rho=4.29$, 95\% Wald interval $[3.87,4.71]$; $\beta=3.97$, $[2.92,5.02]$), the entry stage remains local ($\beta_{\mathrm{ent}}=0.58$, $[0.19,0.97]$), and retention continues to favor previously important topics ($\lambda_{\mathrm{ret}}=0.42$, $[0.11,0.74]$; \cref{fig:split-support-params}). By contrast, the retention-stage popularity term is imprecise and overlaps zero, suggesting that the robust part of the mechanism lies in persistence, local entry, and prior portfolio weight rather than in a strong retention response to topic-wide popularity.

The model's strong fit could in principle be an artifact of later, denser ATS years. To probe that possibility, we repeated the full fitting and simulation exercise after randomly thinning the raw submissions within each year, rebuilding the actor-topic panels each time while holding the pooled concern space fixed. The retain-and-adopt model remains the best simple specification across these thinned corpora: at 40--80\% retention of the raw submissions, mean breadth-tracking correlations remain between $0.78$ and $0.86$, above the single-rule-support alternative ($0.69$--$0.75$), while local-entry rank stays close to the observed value (difference at most $0.016$; \cref{fig:split-support-thinning}). This suggests that the retain-and-adopt result is not simply an artifact of dense late-period data.

\subsection{Pioneering Timing and Regime Position}
To characterize how early agenda entry relates to the regime structure, we define a member-level pioneer index and then relate it to breadth and regime position.

First, we establish a topic emergence year for each policy topic. This is defined as the first year in which the cumulative number of submissions on that topic reaches 15\% of its total historical volume. This metric identifies when a topic transitions from a niche issue to part of the mainstream agenda.

Second, we determine a member entry year for each member-topic pair. Using a 5-year rolling window to smooth out short-term fluctuations, we calculate RPA for every member in every topic for each window. A member is considered to have ``entered'' a topic in the first year its RPA value is greater than or equal to 1.0, signifying specialization.

Third, we calculate a relative pioneer index. For each member-topic specialization, we compute a ``relative lag'': the difference between the member's entry year and the topic's emergence year (or the member's first year of activity, whichever is later). A negative lag indicates pioneering behavior, i.e. adopting a topic before it becomes mainstream, while a positive lag indicates a follower. The pioneer index for a given member is the negative of its average relative lag across all topics it specializes in, such that a higher, positive score corresponds to earlier adoption.

Finally, we summarize each member's regime position using the same three-region partition employed in \cref{fig:portfolio_space_ridgelines}. Let $\omega_{ar}$ denote member $a$'s share of excess specialization signal in regime $r$, and let $m_a=\max_r \omega_{ar}$ denote the member's regime anchoring. We then estimate a simple cross-sectional model
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{PioneerIndex}_a = \alpha + \beta\,\mathrm{Breadth}_a + \lambda\,m_a + \delta_{R(a)} + \varepsilon_a,
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{Breadth}_a$ is the number of specialized topics and $R(a)$ is the member's dominant regime. In the fitted data, both breadth ($\beta=0.26$, $p=0.001$) and regime anchoring ($\lambda=17.70$, $p<0.001$) are positively associated with earlier entry, while dominant-regime indicators are not distinguishable from zero. This is why the main-text pioneer result is framed in terms of regime position and anchoring rather than a distinct ``pioneer type'' attached to one regime alone.

\subsection{Robustness of the Concern Space}
The concern space itself can be stress-tested by removing actors prior to construction and asking how much the resulting topic-topic proximity matrix changes. We do this greedily: at each step, we remove the actor whose exclusion produces the largest root mean squared difference from the current full-space proximity matrix, then repeat. This does not test a substantive behavioral claim directly; it asks whether the shared concern topology is jointly produced or dominated by a small number of actors.

In \cref{fig:sensitivity-analysis}, the concern space changes gradually through much of the removal sequence, indicating that the topology is not driven by any single member. The earliest removals are concentrated among long-established ATS actors and central observer organizations, but the curve steepens only late in the process, when the remaining actor set becomes too thin to sustain the original structure. We therefore interpret the concern space as robustly co-produced: some actors matter more than others, but the geometry is sustained by distributed contributions rather than a lone structural anchor.

That robustness result should be read together with the temporal mosaic in \cref{fig:decadal-space-stack}. The pooled concern space used in the main text is analytically useful, but it remains a long-run composition of historically layered configurations rather than a claim that the latent geometry is literally fixed from decade to decade.

\begin{figure}

  \caption{
    \textbf{Robustness of the concern space to greedy actor removal.} Starting from the full actor set, we iteratively remove the actor whose exclusion produces the largest root mean squared difference between the current proximity matrix and the matrix rebuilt without that actor. The annotated curve therefore reports the most disruptive removal available at each step. The initially gradual increase indicates that the concern space is jointly sustained by many contributors rather than being dominated by a single actor, while the late steepening reflects expected collapse once the remaining actor set becomes too thin to support the original topology.

This pair-level association is exactly what we would expect if co-sponsorship partly tracks shared concern positions. Co-sponsorship therefore provides a useful adjacent view of ATS coordination, but it does not replace the issue-space perspective developed in the main text.

Simple external correlates do less explanatory work. Geographic distance shows no clear negative overlap gradient, EU and NATO pairings are not unusually aligned under permutation, and only BRICS pairs show a positive signal---but on a much smaller ATS sample. These checks therefore point back toward ATS-native positioning rather than broad geography or bloc politics as the more informative lens (\cref{fig:geo-portfolio-overlap,fig:bloc-overlap}).
