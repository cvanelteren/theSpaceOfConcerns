\begin{abstract}
    Governance systems operate through the interplay of formal rules and informal influence, yet this relationship is difficult to quantify. We apply economic-complexity principles to the Antarctic Treaty System (ATS) to map what the regime's actual interests  and how actors navigate them. Adapting the Revealed Comparative Advantage concept to working and information papers that countries produce, we construct a Revealed Policy Advantage measure that normalizes for overall activity and identifies where, rather than how much, states concentrate attention. From these patterns we derive a space of concerns: an interest-space network in which policy topics are linked by co-specialization. We then let interest spread across related topics to estimate each person’s hidden interests and compare them to what they actually work on. The extra difference we measure shows how much someone’s visible activity deviates from what the structure of the topic space would suggestCombining this measure with the breadth of each actor’s revealed portfolio, we identify four roles that describe how states use or underuse their structural position in the issue space: strategic specialists, aligned specialists, structural generalists, and latent generalists. By analyzing these patterns over six decades, we reveal how actor strategies and the structure of concerns dynamically evolve. The analysis uncovers a structured landscape of concerns: a few core topics anchor the regime, and several actors occupy niches that appear narrow on the surface but are structurally far-reaching. This pattern challenges the portrayal of the ATS as a purely scientific collaboration and illustrates how economic-complexity methods reveal underlying tensions and systematic differences in how actors engage.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The Antarctic Treaty System (ATS) stands as one of the most successful examples of international cooperation in modern history, often hailed as the gold standard for global governance and the peaceful resolution of territorial disputes \cite{Aus2025,Yao2021, Yermakova2021}. Established during the Cold War in 1959 by twelve nations and now expanded to 54 parties, it has maintained Antarctica as a zone dedicated to peaceful scientific research for over six decades \cite{Young2011, Zacher1996}.

The system's reputation rests on several remarkable accomplishments: it suspended territorial claims without resolving them, established a nuclear-free zone during the nuclear arms race, and created mechanisms for peaceful dispute resolution that have prevented militarization for over half a century \cite{Haward2012, Koivurova2005, Brady2011}. Covering an area roughly the size of Europe, Antarctica represents one of the most challenging governance environments on Earth—a vast, remote continent rich in strategic interests and untapped resources.

Recent developments, however, reveal the system's complexity. In 2019, while scientists from China, the United States, Russia, and European countries collaborated on climate research at Zhongshan Station, there were rising concerns over China's expanding Antarctic presence \cite{Brady2017}. This duality of visible cooperation and strategic unease is consistent with broader evidence that international institutions often operate through a mix of formal rules, informal practices, and strategic behaviour \cite{Roger2022, Biersteker2024, Vabulas2013, Barnett2021, Stone2013, Slaughter2004}. Rather than treating the ATS as an exception, we draw on this perspective to examine how strategy operates within a regime widely regarded as a diplomatic success \cite{Keohane1984}.

The formal record of the ATS includes treaties, recommendations, and meeting reports, which capture negotiated outcomes but often not the full process by which they emerge.\cite{Gardiner2024,AntarcticTreatyHandbook} Informal corridor conversations, long-standing personal ties, and coordination within and across delegations influence which issues reach the agenda, how they are framed, and which proposals gain momentum, in line with broader analyses of diplomatic grey zones and informal practices in international fora.\cite{GreyZones2023,Saner2005} Because ATS decision-making requires unanimity (consensus by all Consultative Parties), meeting reports typically record only the successful adoption of measures or recommendations, without naming which state(s) objected or withheld support to block consensus\cite{Gardiner2024,CCAMLRConsensus2022,WilsonCenterConsensus2025}. This leaves important aspects of contestation and veto power only partially visible in the record, with attribution to particular states uneven across reports.\cite{Gardiner2024,CCAMLRConsensus2022,WilsonCenterConsensus2025}

At the same time, the record captures both direct acts, such as the submission, co‑sponsorship, and explicit support of working papers and information documents, and indirect acts, such as the absence of support for a proposal or indirect support through measures like (the threat of) withdrawing funding, which scholars of ATS practice and multistakeholder diplomacy treat as important channels of influence.\cite{Gardiner2024,Saner2005,UnofficialDiplomacy2024} These dynamics nonetheless leave traces of overall interests, especially in patterns of sponsorship, support, and non‑support that can be systematically analysed from the documentary record.\cite{Gardiner2024} Existing work on international organizations underscores the importance of such informal practices and hidden influence, but offers limited tools for systematically relating them to observable patterns of institutional activity.\cite{GreyZones2023,Saner2005,Roger2022a,Biersteker2024,Vabulas2013,Barnett2021,Stone2013,Slaughter2004,Koremenos2001}

We take these records to find such traces and recover a more fine-grained picture of what the ATS actually does. We use these formal submissions as observable fingerprints of underlying decision processes. States reveal their interests not only through the topics on which they submit or co-sponsor papers, but also through the topics they systematically ignore. An entity may downplay a politically sensitive concern by concentrating visible activity in neighbouring topics that are closely connected in the concerns space—e.g., by focusing on scientific or environmental dimensions that sit adjacent to more contentious issues of resource access or jurisdiction. In such a representation, topics that lie close together are those that tend to be pursued as a bundle of concerns, while distant topics mark sharper trade-offs.

From these joint patterns of engagement, we construct a \textit{space of concerns}: a low-dimensional representation in which states and topics are positioned according to how they co-occur in treaty practice. In this space, positions and distances summarize revealed priorities and their relationships. Conceptually, this construction is a direct application of the \textit{product space} framework \cite{Hidalgo2007} to international governance: instead of countries and products linked by revealed comparative advantage on exports, we map states and policy domains linked by revealed policy advantage in ATS practice \cite{Beinhocker2006}.

The space of concerns provides three scientific innovations, critical in our analysis. First, it granularizes the Treaty’s broad constitutional narrative into an empirically grounded map of what the regime actually does, topic by topic, translating high-level principles of science, peace, and environmental protection into a concrete landscape of policy work.
Second, it provides a structural context for assessing how specialized or diversified each actor’s engagement is, revealing whether countries occupy narrow niches, central hubs, or the periphery in the issue space, and whether they systematically neglect structurally adjacent concerns.
Third, it underpins our classification of actor roles in strategic specialists, aligned specialists, structural generalists, and latent generalists, by linking revealed policy advantages to the latent opportunities and constraints implied by the topology of the concern network.

Our analysis proceeds in three steps (see methods for details). First, we use revealed policy advantages to map where actors concentrate their submissions and how topics are proximally related in the concern space. Second, we examine the resulting topology to identify clusters of issues and structural roles within the network, showing how some concerns anchor the regime while others remain peripheral. Third, we exploit the geometry of this space to infer latent interests via a diffusion process, comparing actors’ revealed profiles to the structurally implied ones and classifying them into strategic specialists, aligned specialists, structural generalists, and latent generalists. If the Antarctic Treaty System—widely considered the pinnacle of successful international cooperation—exhibits systematic patterns consistent with strategic calculations rather than purely scientific motivations, this should be visible in the structure of the concern space, in the distribution of actor roles, and in how these patterns evolve over time.

By constructing and analysing this space of concerns, we show which actors cluster around similar bundles of issues, how they move through neighbouring topics, which dimensions of policy trade-offs dominate their behaviour, and where latent tensions or potential coalitions are likely to emerge. This representation can be used to anticipate where proposals will encounter resistance or support, to identify states whose stated commitments diverge from their revealed priorities, and to detect cases where actors appear to be preparing the ground for future engagement on more contentious topics by first building activity in adjacent areas. More broadly, the approach offers a generalizable tool for diagnosing the alignment between formal commitments and actual practice in complex governance systems, and for assessing how institutional design and geopolitical change reshape the underlying landscape of concerns.

\section{Results}
\subsection{The space of concerns}
We treat the ATS as a set of agents whose interactions are structured by formal rules and informal norms. The observable actions of these agents—specifically, their submissions of working papers and information documents—provide a record of how they allocate attention across policy domains. We assume these actions reveal underlying priorities and strategic calculations, which may not always align with their official commitments to scientific cooperation and environmental protection.

These submissions provide a rich source of data on how countries prioritize different policy domains. If an agent proposes a working paper on a specific topic, it indicates that the agent has a vested interest in that area, whether it be resource management, territorial claims, or scientific research.

We quantify \textit{how much} interest agents have in those areas using the submission patterns of working papers and information documents. Let $x(a, i)$ denote the number of working papers submitted by agent $a$ on topic $i$ from a list of topics $T$. Because some (typically richer) countries are systematically more active than others, we measure the relative activity on a topic in relation to other topics, which captures \textit{where} an actor concentrates effort rather than simply \textit{how much} it participates. We, then, define the \textit{Revealed Policy Advantage} ($\rho$) for each country and topic as the ratio of those relative activities in relation to other countries:

\begin{dmath} \label{eq:rpa}
  \rho(a,i) = \frac{\pi(a,i)}{Z_i},
\end{dmath}

with $\pi(a,i) = x(a,i) / \sum_j x(a,j)$ indicating the policy focus of agent $a$ on topic $i$ and $Z_i = \frac{\sum_c x(c,i)}{\sum_{c,j} x(c,j)}$ representing the general interest in topic $i$ across all agents.

For values $\rho(a, i) > 1$ an agent has more vested interest in topic $i$ than the other agents in the system. By construction, $\rho(a,i)$ abstracts from absolute activity levels and instead captures the \textit{relative} specialization of each agent across policy domains. This concept is adapted from Revealed Comparative Advantage in economic complexity theory, where countries are evaluated based on their export patterns relative to global trade dynamics \cite{Hidalgo2007}.

Using \cref{eq:rpa}, we construct the relative proximity between topics $i$ and $j$ by considering how likely it is for an agent to be interested in both topics. If an agent with some underlying interests submits in topic $i$, it is more likely that they also submit in another topic $j$ that represents similar interest. We define the proximity between topics $i$ and $j$ as the minimum conditional probability that an agent "specializing" in one topic also specializes in the other:
\begin{dmath} \label{eq:proximity}
\begin{aligned}
\phi(i,j) = \min_c \{
    & P(\rho(c,i) \ge 1 \mid \rho(c,j) \ge 1),\\
    & P(\rho(c,j) \ge 1 \mid \rho(c,i) \ge 1)\}
\end{aligned}
\end{dmath}

This proximity measure captures how likely specialization in one topic is accompanied by specialization in another, revealing the structure of the space of concerns. Taking the minimum of the two conditional probabilities yields a conservative estimate of the relationship and prevents spurious high proximities driven by a single highly specialized agent.


From the topic–topic proximities in \cref{eq:proximity}, we construct a network in which nodes are policy domains and weighted edges represent the strength of their association in treaty practice. This network is the operational form of the space of concerns introduced in the Introduction and corresponds to the product-space-style topology induced by revealed policy advantages.

The space of concerns can be used as a map where traversing from one topic to another represents the shortest path between two policy domains implied by the submission patterns of the agents that constitute the ATS. The space of concerns exhibits a mixed structural pattern with both hierarchical and clustered characteristics. Power centrality shows a roughly normal distribution centered around moderate values, indicating relatively balanced connectivity across the network rather than extreme core-periphery polarization (\cref{fig:centrality-ats}). Eigenvector centrality reveals a distinct pattern with most nodes clustered around moderate values (~0.2) and a small number of highly influential outliers near maximum centrality (1.0), suggesting a few dominant concerns that anchor the network's influence structure. Betweenness centrality displays the expected right-skewed distribution with most nodes having very low betweenness values, confirming that only a small subset of concerns serve as critical bridges connecting different parts of the network. Closeness centrality is concentrated in a relatively narrow range (0.5-0.7), indicating that most concerns maintain similar levels of accessibility within the overall network structure, rather than being evenly distributed across all possible values.

At the top of the heatmap, we observe highly specialized actors with narrow policy focus. In contrast, countries such as the United States, Australia, and the United Kingdom demonstrate broad engagement across multiple topics, reflecting more diverse policy interests and comprehensive involvement in the treaty system.

The heatmap further reveals distinct clusters of countries with aligned policy interests, notably among European Union members and South American countries, suggesting regional coordination patterns within the broader Antarctic governance framework. The marginal panel plots display average activity levels across agents (right panel) and topics (top panel). This suggests that specialized, single-party issues tend to cover topics that the more active core participants address less frequently, creating a complementary structure within the treaty system.



\subsection{Inferring hidden interests}
We assume that each agent’s revealed activity $\rho(a, t)$ is a sample from a latent interest distribution $\theta_a$. This distribution reflects both the agent’s intrinsic priorities and the structural proximity between topics. The \protect\textit{space of concerns} captures this structure: it encodes how topics are related based on patterns of co-submission, and defines how influence or relevance might diffuse between them. That is, we assume that if two topics are closely connected in this space, then an agent active in one is more likely to have latent interest in the other. The revealed policy advantage $\rho(a, .)$ serves as an initial estimate of the agent’s latent interest distribution, such that $\hat{\theta}_a = \rho(a, .)$. Combined with the space of concerns, this forms a prior belief from which we infer a more complete profile of the agent’s interests.

Our goal is to infer $\theta_a$ given $\rho(a, .)$, using the structure of the space of concerns to guide the inference. We begin with an initial estimate

\[
  \hat{\theta}_a = \rho(a, .)
\]

and iteratively update this estimate using a diffusion process defined by

\begin{equation}
  f^{(t+1)}_a = \alpha \hat{\theta}_a + (1 - \alpha) D^{-1} W f^{(t)}_a,
  \label{eq:diffusion}
\end{equation}

until convergence, i.e. when $|f^{(t + 1)}_a - f^{(t)}_a| < \epsilon$, with $\epsilon = 1 \times 10^{-6}$ and $f^{(0)} = \hat{\theta}$. Here, $W$ is the weighted adjacency matrix of the topic proximity network, and $D$ is the diagonal degree matrix. The parameter $\alpha$ controls the balance between retention of the revealed signal and structural diffusion: for $\alpha = 1$, no diffusion occurs and the latent profile is equal to the revealed interest, $f_a = \hat{\theta}_a$. In contrast, for $\alpha = 0$, the resulting distribution $f_a$ reflects only the global structure of the topic space and becomes identical for all agents.

This formulation generalizes the PageRank algorithm, which typically sets $\alpha = 0.85$, and allows us to examine how interest distributions evolve as a function of structural proximity. As $\alpha$ decreases, diffusion progresses further over the structure, smoothing and redistributing the agent's revealed focus into adjacent topics. Agents whose revealed interests are structurally aligned will converge quickly toward the structural equilibrium. In contrast, agents whose interests are concentrated in structurally isolated regions will show slower convergence. This divergence reveals latent affinity to nearby concerns that were not explicitly revealed in their original activity.

To control for singular parties having high activity, we use a one-hot encoder to compute a baseline diffusion profile, $f^0_a$, which is the diffusion of a one-hot vector representing the agent's revealed interest. This baseline serves as a reference point for comparing the agent's latent profile against a focused submission. The excess KL divergence between the latent profile and the RCA, normalized against the baseline diffusion, quantifies how much more the agent's RCA diverges from the structure-induced latent profile than would be expected from a focused submission alone.

\Cref{fig:hidden_interests} illustrates the various actor strategies within the space of concerns. Panel (A) presents a morphospace where actors are positioned based on their policy specialization (measured by the Area Under the Curve (AUC) of excess entropy for $\alpha$) and their policy diversification (measured by the AUC of entropy for $\alpha$). This plot reveals an ``efficient frontier'' that delineates a trade-off between being a \textit{specialist} (e.g., Slovakia) and a \textit{generalist} (e.g., United States). The underlying quantification of ``hidden interests" in this context is operationalized as the excess Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the diffused latent profile and the revealed comparative advantage (RCA) of each agent in the Antarctic Treaty System.

The excess KL divergence is computed as follows: for each agent, we simulate a one-hot RCA vector, representing the most concentrated possible revealed interest. This synthetic baseline is diffused across the space of concerns in the same manner as the true RCA vector. The KL divergence between the diffused one-hot vector and the structure itself serves as a lower-bound reference. The \textit{excess} KL for an agent is then the difference between the KL divergence of their actual profile and that of the one-hot reference. This quantifies how much more an agent’s revealed interests diverge from the structural equilibrium than would be expected from pure specialization alone. High excess KL values thus suggest structurally misaligned or strategically narrow interest profiles, while lower values indicate interests that are already well-aligned with the topology of concerns.

Based on their positions within the morphospace shown in \cref{fig:hidden_interests}(A), we coarsely delineate four stylized classes of actor strategies. Agents characterized by a narrow interest range (low diversification/entropy) and high excess KL divergence (high specialization) are classified as \textit{strategic specialists}. Their revealed interests are focused, but diffusion reveals that they have latent interests in nearby topics that are not reflected in their submissions. \textit{Aligned specialists} also have narrow interests, but their revealed profile aligns closely with the structural equilibrium, showing little divergence after diffusion. \textit{Structural generalists} exhibit broad, well-aligned interests with minimal divergence, while \textit{latent generalists} appear broad in their RCA, yet diffusion reveals even greater latent reach—suggesting overlooked or unrealized structural potential across the issue space. Panel (B) of \cref{fig:hidden_interests} further visualizes the latent interest diffusion process for exemplar actors, demonstrating how engagement ($f_\alpha$, node size) spreads from their revealed interests as the diffusion parameter $\alpha$ increases, showcasing the narrow, concentrated profile of a specialist versus the broad, distributed profile of a generalist.

\section{Discussion}
\subsection{The Antarctic Treaty System as a Space of Concerns}
The Antarctic Treaty System (ATS) has long been celebrated as an institutional achievement that channels geopolitical rivalry into scientific cooperation. Yet as the system has expanded and diversified over more than sixty years, the governance landscape it embodies has become increasingly complex. Our introduction of the \textit{space of concerns} framework provides a way to understand this complexity not simply as a proliferation of issues, but as the emergence of a structured and navigable architecture of policy attention.

By modelling how topics relate to one another and how actors are positioned within this network, the space of concerns offers a lens through which the ATS can be understood as an organised field of strategic behaviour. Rather than treating submissions as isolated signals of interest, the framework highlights the structural environment in which those interests take shape. It reveals that actors operate within a patterned topology of interdependencies, where choices in one domain condition and constrain choices in others. This moves the analysis beyond enumerating what states do and toward understanding the \emph{contexts} that make certain actions more likely, more costly, or more strategically meaningful.

Within this environment, the comparison between an actor’s revealed profile and the structurally implied one---captured through excess KL divergence and diffusion-based latent interest estimates---becomes a way to interrogate how states navigate institutional pressures. High divergence values, for instance, do not diagnose intent, but they do signal places where structural expectations and revealed engagement differ in systematic ways. These divergences help identify points where institutional roles, capacity limitations, or strategic priorities yield behaviour that is not fully aligned with the structural landscape. Such diagnostics thereby contribute to a broader conceptualization of the ATS as a system in which actors negotiate between overt commitments and the latent pull of their embeddedness in the concern space.

Importantly, this approach does not claim that structural relationships alone determine behaviour. Instead, it emphasizes how the ATS constitutes a patterned environment that shapes and channels state engagement, even as domestic priorities, institutional mandates, and geopolitical considerations continue to play a role. The space of concerns thus provides a middle ground: a framework that respects both the agency of actors and the structural forces inherent in long-lived treaty systems.

\subsection{Temporal Dynamics of Actor Strategies}
Bringing a temporal dimension into view further extends this systemic perspective. As the ATS matured, the structure of concerns itself evolved: topics became more interconnected, policy domains expanded, and institutional responsibilities diversified. The trajectories of actors within this shifting environment illustrate how the ATS functions not as a static arrangement but as a continuously adapting governance ecosystem.

Actors do not follow identical paths, but what matters for interpretation is not the specific direction of movement but the existence of movement at all. The long-term shifts in diversification and specialization demonstrate that engagement with Antarctic governance is responsive to changing institutional conditions. These trajectories reflect broader processes: the increasing environmentalization of global politics, the rise of specialized treaty bodies, and the growing recognition that Antarctic governance is inseparable from planetary-scale challenges such as climate change, conservation, and logistics.

In this sense, the temporal patterns show how the ATS gradually transitions from a narrowly scientific arrangement into a multifaceted governance regime. States learn, reposition, specialise, and sometimes reorient as new issues crystallize and old ones decline in salience. The dynamic interplay between actors and the evolving concern space underscores that Antarctic governance is not merely a repository of historical norms; it is a living institutional field where roles and expectations are continuously redefined.

\subsection{Broader Significance}
Taken together, the structural and temporal perspectives developed here point toward a more holistic understanding of the ATS. Rather than asking whether states behave strategically or scientifically, the space-of-concerns framework reveals \emph{how} they operate within a system that blends both logics. It highlights the patterned nature of issue interdependencies, the latent pressures embedded in institutional structure, and the adaptive behaviour of actors over time.

This broader vantage point reframes the ATS as an evolving governance system whose stability emerges not from the absence of strategic behaviour but from the patterned ways in which that behaviour is embedded within the concern space. The framework developed here provides a basis for analysing such dynamics across other international regimes, offering a generalizable way to study how actors navigate complex institutional landscapes, how hidden priorities shape engagement, and how treaty systems evolve as their issue structures deepen and diversify.

Ultimately, this perspective invites a shift from viewing the ATS as a static exemplar of scientific cooperation to seeing it as a dynamic, structured, and adaptive field of global governance—one in which science, strategy, and institutional context continually co-produce the contours of international action.

To construct the space of concerns, we analyze the working papers and information documents submitted to the Antartic Treaty System from 1959 to 2023. The data is collected from the official Antartic Treaty System website and includes all working papers and information documents submitted by treaty members during this period. We focus on the topics covered in these documents, which are categorized into different policy domains such as resource management, territorial claims, scientific research, and environmental protection.

For these documents, a clustering was performed based on the co-submission patterns of working papers and information documents. The clustering algorithm groups topics that are frequently submitted together by the same agents. The Leiden algorithm was then used to identify clusters of agents that share similar policy interests as indicated by their co-submission patterns \cite{Traag2019}.

The co-submission patterns suggest that actors who frequently align on particular topics also tend to maintain consistent collaboration across a wider range of issues. Although nearly all parties engage with the Antarctic Treaty System to some degree, their levels of involvement and patterns of interaction differ substantially.

A particularly active cluster (dark blue) stands out for its sustained engagement across virtually all topics. This group includes the UK, Norway, the US, Australia, New Zealand, and Argentina—nations with long-standing interests in Antarctica and historically strong involvement in its governance. Their interconnections are dense, reflecting tight coordination and repeated co-submissions on a wide array of issues.

A second, also active cluster (green) features countries such as Germany, Belgium, the Netherlands, Poland, and South Korea. While these actors contribute extensively, their collaborative structure is more diffuse: they are broadly interconnected, but without the tightly knit substructures seen in the dark blue group. Their engagement spans many topics, often in coordination with different partners across the cluster.

A third cluster (red) appears more regionally anchored and includes China, India, and several South American countries such as Ecuador and Colombia. These actors are increasingly active and tend to collaborate among themselves, though their submission patterns are somewhat more focused and less evenly distributed across all topics. Their presence suggests growing engagement with the Antarctic governance regime, possibly reflecting expanding national programs or geopolitical interests.

Another notable group (yellow) consists primarily of observer organizations and specialized entities, such as SCAR (Scientific Committee on Antarctic Research), COMNAP (Council of Managers of National Antarctic Programs), and the United Nations bodies. This group plays a crucial role in supporting scientific, logistical, and legal frameworks within the Treaty System. Their contributions are specialized but influential, often underpinning the technical basis for policy discussions.

Finally, there is a smaller and more loosely affiliated set of actors (brown), including several newer or less active consultative parties. Their participation is more selective, with contributions that are often limited to specific areas of expertise or interest. These actors tend to collaborate opportunistically, aligning with other groups on an issue-by-issue basis.

\subsection{Interpreting latent interests and methodological limitations}
\label{sec:latent_interests_methods}
Our approach to inferring latent interests rests on several substantive and methodological assumptions that warrant careful discussion. First, the \textit{space of concerns} is constructed from empirical co-specialization patterns: topics are deemed proximate when actors that specialize in one tend also to specialize in the other. This follows the logic of the product space in economic complexity, where co-export patterns are taken as evidence of shared capabilities \cite{Hidalgo2007}. In the context of the Antarctic Treaty System, however, co-submission may also reflect procedural bundling, institutional mandates, or path-dependent collaboration rather than deep substitutability or complementarity of concerns. The proximity measure therefore encodes observed patterns of joint engagement, not a strong claim about the intrinsic similarity or causal linkage between issues.

Second, the diffusion process used to obtain latent interest profiles models interest as spreading along the topology of the concern space according to a proximity-weighted random walk. This should primarily be understood as a smoothing operator that projects an actor's revealed profile onto the structural neighbourhood implied by its current positions. It does not aim to reproduce the micro-dynamics of negotiation, persuasion, or domestic preference formation. Different choices of the diffusion parameter $\alpha$ control how strongly the latent profile is pulled toward the revealed distribution versus the global structure. While this formulation generalizes PageRank and has desirable mathematical properties, it is nonetheless a modelling choice. Robustness checks with alternative values of $\alpha$ and with alternative proximity specifications are important for assessing how sensitive the inferred latent profiles are to these assumptions.

Third, the notion of \textit{hidden interests} captured by the excess KL divergence is inherently structural and counterfactual. High excess KL values indicate that an actor's revealed profile diverges more from the structurally implied latent profile than would be expected from a purely focused submission in a similar position in the space. This divergence may be consistent with strategically narrow or deliberately understated engagement, but it can equally reflect capacity constraints, domestic political priorities, bureaucratic fragmentation, or normative self-restraint. Our method identifies topics that are structurally adjacent yet under-represented in an actor's observable activity; it does not, on its own, establish whether this under-representation is intentional, temporary, or normatively problematic.

Finally, the use of KL divergence and one-hot baselines introduces additional interpretive choices. We compute divergence between the diffused latent distribution and the revealed policy advantage, with a synthetic baseline that represents the diffusion of a maximally concentrated submission profile. This provides a conservative lower bound for the divergence that we would expect from specialization alone. However, KL divergence is asymmetric and sensitive to low-probability events, so we apply appropriate smoothing to avoid artefacts driven by zero entries. Taken together, these design choices mean that the latent profiles and excess KL values should be read as indicators of structural alignment or misalignment with the concern space, rather than as direct measurements of true but concealed preferences. Substantive interpretation, particularly in case studies, should therefore combine these quantitative diagnostics with qualitative knowledge of actors' capabilities, institutional mandates, and documented positions in the Antarctic Treaty System.

\subsection{Sensitivity Analysis}
To evaluate the stability of the observed structure, we performed a sensitivity analysis by removing each country from the space of concerns and computing the root mean squared error between the proximity matrix of the full space and the space without that country. The process is repeated by iteratively removing the agent that produces the maximal difference between the current set of countries and the set of countries without that agent. This greedy approach would help us understand what each agent contributes to the construction of the space of concerns in Antartica.

In \cref{fig:sensitivity-analysis}, we observe that the space of concerns is robust against the removal of a large fraction of countries. This is evidenced by the relatively gradual increase in spectral distance observed through the first 60\% of removals, suggesting that the global structure of the space is maintained even when many individual contributors are excluded. This robustness implies a degree of redundancy or modularity in how countries co-structure the space, where no single actor—apart from a few key exceptions—critically determines the overall topology.

However, we also observe distinct inflection points in the curve, where the removal of specific countries results in sharp increases in spectral distance. Notably, countries such as China, Germany, France, and the United Kingdom contribute disproportionately to the coherence of the product space. Their removal induces substantial distortions in the spectral representation, indicating their role as structural anchors or bridges across different regions of the space. In contrast, even countries with large economic output, such as the United States and India, show a more subdued effect on the global geometry, underscoring that structural importance is not merely a function of scale but also of positionality within the network.

Toward the final stages of the removal sequence, the spectral distance rapidly approaches its maximum, signifying a collapse of the original structure. Interestingly, some of the countries responsible for this final increase—such as Sweden, Venezuela, and Romania—are not necessarily central by economic metrics but appear to occupy topologically unique or peripheral positions that preserve fragile clusters or minor components.

Overall, the analysis highlights the asymmetric role of countries in shaping the global structure of the space of concerns. While many actors contribute incrementally, a small subset exerts an outsized influence on the cohesion and organization of the space. This spectral sensitivity analysis thus offers a principled approach for identifying structurally pivotal agents beyond conventional economic indicators.
