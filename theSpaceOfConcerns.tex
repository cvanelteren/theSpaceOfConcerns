\begin{abstract}
    The Antarctic Treaty System (ATS) is a diplomatic anomaly: it suspended territorial claims and prevented militarization for sixty years while governing a continent rich in strategic interests. Yet its strategic architecture remains opaque because consensus rules hide dissent and formal records appear technocratic. We apply economic-complexity tools to six decades of working papers, treating submissions as fingerprints of latent strategy. We build a ``space of concerns'': a network where policy topics are linked by co-sponsorship. States often bundle low-salience scientific topics with adjacent contentious ones, signaling intent through composition rather than volume. Archetypal analysis identifies three modes—Specialists, Moderate Specialists, and Aligned Generalists—and the regime moves from early volatility to a structured, self-reinforcing ecosystem. As participation broadens, structural alignment tightens, challenging the notion that complexity drifts toward entropy. The framework recovers the latent topology of diplomatic engagement and offers a portable blueprint for decoding strategic evolution in other consensus-based regimes.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Antarctica is an institutional paradox: an uninhabited continent where seven nations asserted overlapping territorial claims despite limited legal basis for occupation. From this configuration emerged the Antarctic Treaty System (ATS), which has frozen territorial disputes and prevented militarization for over six decades. The regime operates through deliberate ambiguity—sovereignty claims are neither recognized nor relinquished—making it difficult to observe how agendas are set, who wields influence, and how competition is expressed.

International governance often appears rules-based, yet formal procedures are braided with competing interests, informal practices, and hidden vetoes \cite{Stone2013,Biersteker2024,Vabulas2013,Barnett2021,Slaughter2004}. The ATS exemplifies this opacity: consensus rules and diplomatic choreography mask who shapes the agenda, who blocks, and how strategy moves \cite{AntarcticTreatyHandough,Gardiner2024}. To reveal the system beneath the process, we reconstruct its issue network from what states submit, co-sponsor, and ignore, treating working papers as traces of positioning \cite{Gardiner2024}.

Submissions, co-sponsorships, and silences offer observable fingerprints of strategy. States reveal priorities not only by where they act, but by what they systematically avoid—often shifting visible effort to adjacent, less contentious topics. Building on economic complexity, we adapt the ``product space'' to governance: a revealed policy advantage links jointly specialized topics into a \textit{space of concerns}, a structural map where distance encodes co-specialization rather than text \cite{Hidalgo2007, Beinhocker2006}. Diffusing interest across this network infers latent engagement and locates actors relative to the regime’s opportunity landscape.

We analyze the complete corpus of working papers and information documents submitted to the ATS between 1959 and 2023. Rather than treating issues as isolated agenda items, we construct a network of interrelated concerns that reveals the hidden dependencies linking disparate policy domains. This topological map uncovers three features of the regime. First, the governance architecture is structured by specific anchors and bridges that constrain how attention can move between issues. Second, while engagement breadth expands over six decades, structural alignment tightens, suggesting maturation of strategic norms rather than drift toward entropy. Third, actors navigate this web through compositional modes—aligned generalists, peripheral specialists, and transitional profiles. The resulting framework offers a portable blueprint for decoding how the connectivity of issues shapes strategic evolution in consensus-based regimes.

\subsection{The Geometry of Diplomatic Attention}
We begin by constructing the aggregate \emph{space of concerns} (\cref{fig:space_of_concerns}[A]), pooling six decades of submissions (1959-2023) to uncover the regime's persistent structural constraints. The network shown is the maximum spanning tree over topic proximities with the strongest links overlaid (edges at or above the 95th percentile), so the figure retains the backbone while highlighting exceptionally close couplings. This map defines the regime's strategic terrain where nodes represent strategic interests and edges encode the proximity between those topics. Two topics are connected if states frequently specialize in them simultaneously, so adjacency reflects co-specialization and coalition overlap rather than semantic similarity. The aggregate view therefore captures the long-run constraints within which actors can reposition without rewriting the treaty’s procedural grammar.

The ATS issue map reveals a highly structured ecosystem rather than a random web. A dense core of interconnected topics anchors day-to-day governance, including legal matters, inspections, information exchange, and meeting formalities such as opening statements and workplans. This procedural core acts as a shared gateway: even when actors disagree on substantive issues, they still converge on inspections, reporting, and meeting rules because these are the common access points to the regime. From this core, three main branches extend toward resource extraction (mining, drilling, marine acoustics), biological prospecting, and safety and operations, indicating where specialized attention accumulates once access through the core is secured.

The geometry also shows a separation between routine administration and episodic pressure points. Core topics are tightly interlinked and persist across the network, while peripheral branches are thinner and more linear, signaling that entry into these domains is more contingent and coalition-dependent. In the aggregate, the space thus resembles a core-periphery: routine procedural work holds the regime together, and contentious or specialized issues attach as satellites that actors approach through the core rather than in isolation.

Mapping actors onto this terrain (\cref{fig:actors_on_space}) shows clustered, asymmetric contributions. Panels A/B (aggregate) reveal four Leiden communities: a dominant blue core of longstanding consultative parties with permanent stations and logistics (e.g., United States, United Kingdom, Australia) that spans governance anchors and operations (blue dominates most topic bars); a red community mixing organizations (IPY-IPO, IUCN) with mid-activity or newer programs (e.g., Turkey, Bulgaria, Czech Republic, Malaysia) that takes the largest share on cooperation-with-other-organizations and adjacent governance items; a purple group anchored by smaller, education-focused programs (e.g., Peru, Uruguay) centered on the educational-issues callout; and a green cluster led by science-intensive programs (China, India, Russia) that targets a narrow science/operations niche around sub-glacial lakes and environmental domains analysis.

Panel C embeds these clusters into the space of concerns, marking each cluster’s highest relative-interest topics (RCA $\geq 1$) with dashed circles, and panel D tracks how dominance shifts over time. We can coarsely delineate between four types engagement modes based on where each cluster concentrates its influence.

While panel C shows where clusters sit in the space of concerns, panel D reveals how dominance in topics over time.

\textbf{Backbone cluster (blue).} The blue cluster maintains broad dominance across governance and operations, with only a modest dip in the 1990s as new entrants expand participation. Foundational topics—marine acoustics, management plans, inspections, and opening statements—remain blue-led throughout, indicating that early signatories anchor the procedural spine even as participation broadens.

\textbf{Coordination cluster (red).} The red community surges in the early 2000s around cooperation with other organizations, planning, and status reports, coinciding with the International Polar Year and tourism guidelines. The mix of time-bounded coordination bodies (IPY-IPO) and smaller or newer national programs aligns with this timing: their influence concentrates when cross-institutional coordination becomes salient, then tapers as those agenda items recede.

\textbf{Educational/procedural cluster(purple).} The purple group peaks in the late 1990s/early 2000s on educational and adjacent procedural issues. Many of these actors run seasonal or small-footprint programs (e.g., Uruguay’s Artigas base, Peru’s Machu Picchu station), which aligns with a strategy of targeted visibility through education and outreach rather than broad operational control.

\textbf{Science/extraction cluster (green).} The green cluster gains traction in the 2010s on science and resource-extraction niches—drilling, sub-glacial lakes, biological prospecting—aligning with the expansion of large national programs (China’s multi-station network, India’s Maitri/Bharati, Russia’s long-standing multi-station presence). Its appearances are sharp and bounded, reflecting targeted pushes into specific opportunity zones rather than broad agenda control.

While the blue cluster is dominant, other clusters show a greater intensity in interest in specific clusters, indicating that influence is both concentrated and distributed depending on the topic. The regime thus exhibits a layered structure where a procedural core held by longstanding actors, with specialized branches where newer or niche programs can assert influence through targeted engagement.

By casting topic submissions into a space of concerns, we recover a latent topology that structures how actors can engage and makes the core content of the ATS legible at a granular level. The regime is anchored by procedural cores that act as shared access points, while specialized branches extend into contentious or niche domains. Actor contributions cluster into distinct communities that map onto this topology, with agenda control shifting predictably as institutional windows open and close. The results show a regime organized by a durable issue backbone, with clustered roles that evolve in bursts rather than drift. Participation broadens, but the structure of attention does not diffuse: actors diversify within the constraints of the core, peripheral agendas flare when institutional or scientific windows open, and archetypal strategies capture the dominant modes of engagement. The ATS thus scales without redistributing influence, expanding along the same structural pathways that anchor the system.

We next move to actor behavior analysis, tracking how actors traverse this space over time alongside system-level shifts in topology.

While the aggregated network reveals the geometry of concerns, its topology is not static. \Cref{fig:space_of_concerns}[B] tracks key network metrics over rolling 5-year windows, revealing phases in the regime's evolution. The early years (1959--1990s) show relatively steady participation but low cohesion between topics, reflecting a foundational period dominated by a few core actors. The 1990s and 2000s bring rapid expansion in active members and link density, coinciding with the Madrid Protocol and tourism guidelines. By the late 2000s, the giant component peaks and link density eases, aligning with Ross Sea MPA negotiations that introduced new topics and actors and modest fragmentation. Even as overall connectivity dips, betweenness, closeness, and modularity remain remarkably consistent across time (\cref{fig:temporal-centrality-ats} in the Appendix), indicating no sharp structural break during the major policy windows.

This structural topology defines the constraints within which states must operate. We position actors in this landscape using two complementary metrics: Diversity (the breadth of topics a state engages with) and Specialism (the degree to which their focus deviates from the regime's structural core).

As shown in \cref{fig:space_of_concerns}[D], actors populate a clear trade-off frontier. Generalists occupy the high-Diversity region, engaging broadly across central anchors (e.g., United States, United Kingdom, Australia), while specialists concentrate on specific clusters (e.g., Switzerland and newer entrants on peripheral branches). Yet, a state's strategic footprint extends beyond its explicit submissions. By applying a diffusion process (varying the parameter $\alpha$), we infer \emph{latent interests}—topics that are not explicitly targeted but are structurally implied by a state's focus. \Cref{fig:space_of_concerns}[E] illustrates this mechanic: as diffusion relaxes, influence spreads from narrow interventions into adjacent domains, exposing the coherent policy bundles that underpin isolated acts.

\subsection{Systemic Maturation and Structural Convergence}
The aggregate space defined above captures the regime's persistent structural constraints—the collective footprint of six decades of cooperation. Having located actors within this topology, we now examine how they move through it over time. By tracking engagement patterns in sliding windows ($n=5$ years), we observe a clear evolutionary signal: system-wide engagement broadens, yet structural alignment tightens.

\Cref{fig:diffusion_time} reveals that Diversity rises significantly, particularly following the 1991 Environmental Protocol, which expanded the regime's functional scope. Crucially, this expansion does not lead to fragmentation. Instead, the median Specialism drifts downward while its spread narrows (narrowing interquartile range, \cref{fig:diffusion_time}[B]), indicating that as the policy portfolio widens, actors are converging on a more consistent set of structural engagement modes. The regime is becoming more complex, but also more coherent.

This convergence, however, does not imply homogeneity. The actor trajectories in \cref{fig:diffusion_time}[C] reveal distinct, persistent strategies within this tightening envelope. Long-standing generalists like the United States, United Kingdom, and Australia anchor the high-Diversity/low-Specialism frontier. In contrast, emerging or niche actors often enter through high-Specialism peripheral zones before drifting toward the center (e.g., Russia’s shift toward broader engagement) or remaining structurally distinct (e.g., Switzerland). The system thus integrates new entrants by channeling them along specific structural paths rather than diluting the established order.

These shifts align with major institutional milestones. The early expansion in Diversity and the subsequent tightening of Specialism coincide with the post‑1991 Environmental Protocol era, when environmental governance broadened the issue repertoire while consolidating procedural norms. Later increases in participation and agenda complexity map onto growth in consultative parties and a denser set of subsidiary bodies, which deepened the core agenda without fracturing the structural envelope.

These patterned trajectories motivate a compositional view of strategy: do the observed paths cluster into a small set of dominant modes?

\subsection{Archetypes as Modes of Engagement}
Finally, we ask if the diversity of actor paths can be summarized by a few dominant strategies. Archetypal analysis reveals three coherent modes of engagement that span the extremes of the data cloud, acting as structural directions rather than rigid clusters.

We retain $K=3$ archetypes because reconstruction error falls steeply from $K=2$ to $K=3$ and flattens thereafter (\cref{fig:archetype-reconstruction}). As shown in \cref{fig:archetypes}, these archetypes correspond to distinct logics of engagement:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Aligned Generalists} (Archetype 1): High-diversity actors with lower specialism who engage broadly with the regime’s core anchors.
    \item \textbf{Transitional Profiles} (Archetype 2): High-diversity actors with rising specialism, indicating movement toward more focused engagement.
    \item \textbf{Peripheral Specialists} (Archetype 3): Higher-specialism actors with moderate diversity, concentrating effort in distinct niches.
\end{enumerate}

Two additional patterns underscore the interpretive value of this decomposition. First, the trajectories in \cref{fig:archetypes}[B] show that movement is not random: actors tend to travel along a narrow corridor that links aligned-generalist profiles to more specialized ones, indicating that the system’s constraints channel adaptation toward a small number of feasible paths. Second, the diversity-based selection in \cref{fig:archetypes}[A] reveals that the most distinct trajectories still remain within the triangular envelope, suggesting that heterogeneity expresses itself through different mixtures rather than departure from the underlying structural grammar.

Crucially, strategic roles are compositional: most actors do not belong exclusively to one class but mix these modes over time. The ternary trajectories (\cref{fig:archetypes}[A]) show that movement is highly structured: actors rarely drift randomly but tend to slide along the edge between Generalist and Aligned Specialist modes, with Archetype 3 acting as a gateway. Russia, for instance, shifts toward the Archetype 1--2 edge over time, while Switzerland remains Archetype-3 heavy. This suggests that while the regime allows for heterogeneity, the successful pathways to influence are topologically constrained.

\subsection{Strategic Behavior Dynamics: Beyond Geometric Position}

While archetypal decomposition reveals structural directions in the Diversity--Specialism space, understanding strategic behavior requires analyzing \emph{how actors move} through this space over time, not merely where they are positioned. We extend the analysis beyond geometric archetypes to identify coarse strategic behaviors using three complementary methods: dynamic regime classification, trajectory clustering, and phase space transition analysis.

\textbf{Dynamic Regime Classification.} We classify each actor's temporal trajectory by its dynamic properties: volatility (variability in movement speed), directionality (correlation between position and time indicating monotonic shifts), tortuosity (path length relative to displacement), and cyclicity (periodic patterns in velocity). This reveals four dominant behavioral regimes:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Stable actors} (38\%): Maintain consistent strategic positions with low volatility and minimal displacement. These actors exhibit institutional inertia and locked-in strategies.
    \item \textbf{Mixed actors} (39\%): Show moderate volatility and balanced evolution. Examples include the United States and New Zealand, demonstrating adaptive but not erratic responses.
    \item \textbf{Directional actors} (18\%): Undergo clear monotonic strategic shifts with high directionality and low tortuosity. Portugal, SCAR, and China exemplify deliberate repositioning—China's trajectory reflects its growing Antarctic engagement since the 1980s.
    \item \textbf{Volatile actors} (5\%): Display rapid, unpredictable changes. The Antarctic Treaty Secretariat (ATS), Norway, and Peru show reactive strategies with high volatility, reflecting organizational responses to system-wide dynamics.
\end{enumerate}

Notably, the most tortuous trajectories belong to Greece (tortuosity = 756), IMO (362), and ATS (242)—actors that explore many positions but return close to their starting point, suggesting exploratory strategies that ultimately stabilize.

\textbf{Trajectory Clustering and Phase Space Structure.} Clustering actors by trajectory shape and dynamics (using 13 features including position statistics, velocity, displacement, and tortuosity) identifies five groups with similar strategic pathways. This reveals ``who moves like whom'' independent of geometric position. Complementing this, partitioning the phase space into six regions and computing transition probabilities uncovers the system's attractor structure: two regions exhibit high self-transition probability ($\sim$0.85), acting as stable attractors where actors converge and remain, while other regions serve as transition zones with higher outflow.

The streamline visualization overlaid on the ternary plot in \cref{fig:archetypes}[A] shows the velocity field computed from all actor trajectories, revealing collective flows through the strategic space. Strong flows converge toward the Aligned Generalist--Transitional Profile corridor, confirming that most evolutionary pathways follow this channel. Divergent flows are rare, appearing only in peripheral specialist regions where volatile actors explore before stabilizing. Detailed regime classification, trajectory clustering results, and phase space transition matrices are provided in \cref{fig:strategic-regimes,fig:strategic-clusters,fig:strategic-phase-space}.

\textbf{Temporal Flow and Constrained Pathways.} The combination of regime classification, clustering, and phase space analysis reveals that despite high collinearity in geometric position (R$^2$ = 0.73 between diversity and specialism metrics), strategic behavior exhibits rich heterogeneity. Movement matters as much as position: stable actors and directional actors may occupy similar regions but differ fundamentally in dynamics. The phase space structure channels adaptation—actors can explore, but viable pathways are topologically constrained. This explains how the ATS maintains pluralism while exhibiting convergence: diversity expresses itself through different temporal patterns within a bounded strategic landscape.

These findings underscore that strategic roles are dynamic and compositional. Actors do not follow fixed archetypes but navigate between them through trajectories shaped by both internal priorities and systemic constraints. The regime's maturation corresponds to a narrowing of feasible pathways: early volatility gives way to structured flows along established corridors, suggesting the system has developed an ``immune response'' to disruptive strategies.

\subsection{Proportional Expansion: Participation Without Redistribution}
An examination of topic-level competition dynamics (\cref{fig:competition-appendix}) reveals an empirical pattern: while the fraction of countries with RCA $\geq$ 1 has increased—particularly in operational and governance topics where participation reaches 40--50\% of countries after 2000—the Gini coefficients of RCA distributions remain stable around 0.25--0.30 across six decades. This decoupling between participation breadth and distributional inequality suggests \emph{proportional expansion}: new entrants acquire comparative advantage in topics without substantially altering the relative positions of existing participants. The distributional structure persists as the number of active participants grows, maintaining inequality levels even as absolute engagement scales. This pattern is consistent with constrained adaptation—expansion occurs along established gradients rather than redistribution, such that increased participation coexists with stable hierarchical structure.


\section{Discussion}
Our results challenge portrayals of the Antarctic Treaty System as a ``technocratic blur''—an opaque bureaucracy governed primarily by scientific consensus. Instead, we reveal a highly structured strategic ecosystem where influence is exerted through deliberate navigation of an issue topology. Stability in the ATS arises not from the absence of political competition, but from the channeling of that competition into patterned bundles of concerns.

This ``geometry of governance'' has implications for how the regime matures. The shift from early volatility to tight structural alignment suggests that the ATS has evolved a robust ``immune system'' against fragmentation. By anchoring the agenda around central hubs like \textit{marine living resources} and \textit{workplans}, the regime forces actors—even those with divergent interests—to engage through a shared structural core. Bridges like \textit{inspections} (high betweenness) do not merely connect topics; they act as the system's glue, ensuring that technical compliance remains linked to high-politics diplomacy.

Strategic behavior in this landscape is compositional, not categorical. The three archetypes—\emph{Aligned Generalists, Peripheral Specialists}, and \emph{Transitional Profiles}—suggest that states maximize influence by optimizing their position on the Diversity--Specialism frontier. However, our dynamic regime analysis reveals that strategic behavior cannot be reduced to geometric position alone. While 77\% of actors exhibit Stable or Mixed trajectories, maintaining consistent positions or showing moderate adaptive evolution, a critical minority (18\%) follows Directional pathways of deliberate repositioning (exemplified by China's growing engagement) and 5\% display Volatile patterns of rapid strategic shifts (notably the ATS Secretariat, which responds reactively to system-wide dynamics). The tightening of feasible pathways over six decades implies a ``learning curve'': as the regime matures, the space for idiosyncratic or purely disruption-based strategies narrows, pushing entrants and rising powers to adopt the structural grammar of the established order to gain legitimacy.

The phase space transition analysis underscores this constraint. Two attractor regions with high self-transition probability ($\sim$0.85) act as stable equilibria where actors converge and remain, while the streamline visualization reveals that collective flows channel through the Aligned Generalist--Transitional corridor. This topological structure explains why movement through strategic space is highly patterned: actors do not drift randomly but follow established pathways shaped by the regime's architecture. Even volatile actors like Greece and IMO, despite tortuous exploration (tortuosity $>$300), ultimately return close to their starting positions, suggesting that the system permits experimentation but constrains long-term divergence.

Taken together, these results suggest that regime evolution is best understood as constrained adaptation rather than open-ended diversification. The available strategic repertoire expands in breadth, but feasible transitions between modes remain limited; actors converge not because they become identical, but because the network of concerns filters viable pathways. Crucially, despite high collinearity in static geometric position (R$^2$ = 0.73), rich behavioral heterogeneity emerges through temporal dynamics—movement matters as much as position. This framing explains how pluralism can coexist with stability and why the ATS exhibits resilience even as participation scales: diversity expresses itself through different temporal patterns within a bounded strategic landscape.

Beyond Antarctica, this framework offers a generalized map of governance for opaque regimes. Where formal records obscure the intent behind consensus, the space of concerns recovers the hidden architecture of participation, revealing not only where actors position themselves but how they move through constrained pathways. This dual perspective—geometric structure plus temporal dynamics—offers a portable blueprint for decoding how institutional design shapes strategic evolution in complex multilateral settings.

\section{Methods}
\subsection{Data}
We analyze working papers and information documents submitted to the Antarctic Treaty System from 1959 to 2023. The dataset was compiled from the official ATS document archive \cite{Yaryab2024} and enriched with thematic labels, submitting parties, and document types. Documents submitted by the ATS Secretariat itself were retained for completeness.

\subsection{Constructing the Space of Concerns}
Let $x(a,i)$ denote the number of submissions by agent $a$ on topic $i$. We define the normalized activity share
\begin{equation}\label{eq:activity_share}
\pi(a,i) = \frac{x(a,i)}{\sum_j x(a,j)},
\end{equation}

and the system-wide importance of topic $i$ as

\begin{equation}\label{eq:topic_importance}
Z_i = \frac{\sum_c x(c,i)}{\sum_{c,j} x(c,j)}.
\end{equation}

The Revealed Policy Advantage (RPA) is then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rpa}
    \rho(a,i) = \frac{\pi(a,i)}{Z_i}.
\end{equation}

Topics are linked by proximity
\begin{equation}\label{eq:proximity}
\begin{aligned}
\phi(i,j)
&= \min \Bigl\{\\
    &P\bigl(\rho(c,i)\ge 1 \mid \rho(c,j)\ge 1\bigr),\\
    &P\bigl(\rho(c,j)\ge 1 \mid \rho(c,i)\ge 1\bigr)\\
\Bigr\}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
yielding a weighted topic network.
We construct both an aggregate space (for the regime-wide map) and time-resolved spaces by rebuilding proximities within each time window, allowing the topology itself to evolve rather than assuming a fixed structure over time.

\subsection{Latent Interest Diffusion}

Latent interests are inferred via diffusion:
\[
f^{(t+1)}_a = \alpha \hat{\theta}_a + (1-\alpha) D^{-1} W f^{(t)}_a,
\]
where $W$ is the proximity matrix and $\alpha$ controls retention of revealed signal. We report results integrated over $\alpha$ (averaging across a grid), so inference does not hinge on a single diffusion setting.

Specialism is quantified as excess KL divergence relative to a one-hot baseline diffusion.

\subsection{Archetype Weights Over Time}
To relate archetypal trajectories to individual actors, we estimate time-varying mixture weights that express each actor's position as a convex combination of the archetypes. At each time step $t$, let $A_t \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times 2}$ collect the archetype coordinates in Diversity--Specialism space and let $x_{a,t} \in \mathbb{R}^{2}$ denote actor $a$'s coordinates. We solve a least-squares fit $A_t^\top w_{a,t} \approx x_{a,t}$ and transform the coefficients with a softmax to enforce non-negativity and unit sum. This yields weights $w_{a,t}$ that can be tracked over time and plotted in barycentric (ternary) coordinates as a trajectory of archetype mixtures.
For the ternary panel in \cref{fig:archetypes}[A], we display a subset of actors chosen to maximize trajectory diversity: trajectories are resampled to a common length and selected via greedy farthest-point sampling using mean pointwise distance, yielding five maximally distinct paths. Flags mark the final positions for these selected actors. The archetypes shown are drawn from the best-fitting solution among 100 random initializations, chosen by minimal reconstruction error (see \cref{fig:archetype-reconstruction}). We select $K=3$ at the elbow of the decay curve: the median error drops by 22.29\% from $K=2$ to $K=3$, versus 20.31\% from $K=3$ to $K=4$.

\subsection{Strategic Behavior Classification}

Beyond geometric archetypes, we identify behavioral regimes by analyzing trajectory dynamics in 5-year rolling windows. For each actor, we compute:

\textbf{Volatility:} $\sigma_v = \text{std}(\|\mathbf{v}_t\|)$ where $\mathbf{v}_t$ is the velocity vector at time $t$.

\textbf{Directionality:} $d = |\text{corr}(t, \|\mathbf{x}_t - \mathbf{x}_0\|)|$ measuring correlation between time and displacement from origin.

\textbf{Tortuosity:} $\tau = \frac{\sum_t \|\mathbf{x}_{t+1} - \mathbf{x}_t\|}{\|\mathbf{x}_T - \mathbf{x}_0\|}$ comparing path length to net displacement.

Actors are classified into regimes based on these metrics: Stable (low $\sigma_v$, low displacement), Volatile (high $\sigma_v$), Directional (high $d$, low $\tau$), or Mixed (intermediate values).

For trajectory clustering, we extract 13 features per actor: position statistics (mean and standard deviation of entropy and KL excess), dynamics (mean and standard deviation of velocity), shape (displacement, path length, tortuosity), and boundary conditions (start and end positions). K-means clustering with $K=5$ on standardized features groups actors with similar strategic pathways.

Phase space regions are identified by clustering all (actor, year) observations using K-means with $K=6$. The transition probability matrix $P_{ij}$ records the empirical probability of moving from region $i$ to region $j$ in successive time steps, computed by pooling transitions across all actor trajectories. Self-transition probabilities $P_{ii}$ quantify regional stability.

The velocity field for streamline visualization is computed by binning trajectory segments into a regular grid and interpolating velocity vectors using linear griddata interpolation. Streamlines are then generated using standard flow integration, showing collective flows through the strategic landscape.

\subsection{Interpreting Latent Interests and Methodological Limits}
Concern-space proximities capture observed co-specialization patterns; they can reflect procedural bundling or path-dependent collaboration, not only intrinsic topical similarity. The diffusion operator smooths revealed profiles into their structural neighbourhoods and should be read as a projection, not a behavioural model of negotiation. Excess Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence flags misalignment between revealed and structure-implied profiles but does not diagnose intent—capacity constraints, domestic priorities, or self-restraint can yield similar gaps. KL asymmetry and sensitivity to low probabilities are handled with smoothing and a one-hot baseline, giving a conservative lower bound on divergence expected from pure specialization. Quantitative diagnostics should therefore be paired with qualitative knowledge of mandates and capabilities.

Diffusion is structural rather than causal; Specialism signals concentration or misalignment, not deception; and ``latent'' interest refers to structure-implied engagement, not unobserved preferences. Similarly, behavioral regime classification captures temporal patterns in trajectories but does not directly infer strategic intent—volatile behavior may reflect responsiveness, capacity constraints, or institutional role rather than deliberate exploration. These caveats bound the interpretive claims of the analysis.

\subsection{Sensitivity Analysis}
To evaluate the stability of the observed structure, we performed a sensitivity analysis by removing each country from the space of concerns and computing the root mean squared error between the proximity matrix of the full space and the space without that country. The process is repeated by iteratively removing the country that produces the maximal difference between the current set of countries and the set of countries without that country. This greedy approach helps us understand what each country contributes to the construction of the space of concerns in Antarctica.

In \cref{fig:sensitivity-analysis}, we observe that the space of concerns is robust against the removal of a large fraction of countries. This is evidenced by the relatively gradual increase in spectral distance observed through the first 60\% of removals, suggesting that the global structure of the space is maintained even when many individual contributors are excluded. This robustness implies a degree of redundancy or modularity in how countries co-structure the space, where no single actor—apart from a few key exceptions—critically determines the overall topology.

In contrast, even countries with large economic output, such as the United States and India, show a more subdued effect on the global geometry, underscoring that structural importance is not merely a function of scale but also of positionality within the network.

Interestingly, some of the countries responsible for this final increase—such as Sweden, Venezuela, and Romania—are not necessarily central by economic metrics but appear to occupy topologically unique or peripheral positions that preserve fragile clusters or minor components.

Overall, the analysis highlights the asymmetric role of countries in shaping the global structure of the space of concerns. While many actors contribute incrementally, a small subset exerts an outsized influence on the cohesion and organization of the space. This spectral sensitivity analysis thus offers a principled approach for identifying structurally pivotal actors beyond conventional economic indicators.
